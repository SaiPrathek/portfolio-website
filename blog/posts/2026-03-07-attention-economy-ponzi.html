The Attention Economy Is a Ponzi Scheme (And We're All Invested)

MEMO #021 · 07 Mar 2026 · 12 min read

The Attention Economy Is a Ponzi Scheme (And We're All Invested)

Attention is the new currency, but the supply is fixed and every app wants more. The maths doesn't work — and AI might make it worse.

AttentionTechCulture

Let me tell you about a currency that's more valuable than the rupee, more fought-over than Bitcoin, and more finite than gold. You can't mine more of it. You can't print more of it. You can't borrow it, invest it, or earn interest on it. When it's gone, it's gone — and every single day, you wake up with exactly the same amount.

It's your attention.

You have approximately 16 waking hours per day. That's 960 minutes. That's 57,600 seconds. And every app on your phone, every notification on your watch, every email in your inbox, every billboard on your commute, every YouTube thumbnail, every push alert, every "you might also like" recommendation is fighting — viciously, with billions of rupees in R&D behind it — for a share of those 57,600 seconds.

The supply is fixed. The demand is infinite. And every year, more competitors enter the market.

This is not a functioning economy. This is a Ponzi scheme.

How a Ponzi Scheme Works (In Case You've Been Too Busy Scrolling to Learn)

A quick refresher, because the analogy only works if you understand both halves.

A Ponzi scheme works like this: early investors put money in and receive enormous returns. These returns aren't generated by any actual productive activity — they're paid out of the money contributed by later investors. As long as new money keeps flowing in, everyone thinks the system is working. Returns are great. Confidence is high. Early investors tell their friends. More money flows in.

The problem is mathematical: the scheme requires exponentially growing inputs to sustain linearly growing payouts. Eventually, inevitably, the inputs can't keep up. The pool runs dry. The whole thing collapses. The early investors walk away rich. Everyone else gets a notice from a lawyer.

Now replace "money" with "attention" and watch what happens.

The Early Investors: Email, Google, Facebook

In the early 2000s, the attention economy was wide open. The internet was young. Smartphones didn't exist. The average person had email, maybe a search engine, maybe one or two websites they visited regularly. The total demand on their digital attention was modest.

Into this landscape walked Google, Facebook, and the first generation of social media. They were the early investors, and the returns were spectacular. Google captured search attention — the moments when you actively wanted information. Facebook captured social attention — the moments when you wanted to know what your friends were doing. Email captured professional attention — the moments when you needed to communicate.

Each of these platforms carved out a substantial slice of the attention pie, and there was plenty of pie to go around. People had hours of uncommitted attention sitting idle. The early platforms simply redirected time that was previously spent staring out of windows, reading physical newspapers, or experiencing what psychologists call "boredom" and what we now recognise as an extinct emotional state, like the dodo of feelings.

The returns were so good that everyone wanted in. And so the second wave arrived: Twitter, Instagram, Snapchat, streaming services, podcasts, push notifications, smartwatches, in-app alerts. Then the third wave: TikTok, Substacks, Discord servers, group chats, AI chatbots, short-form video everywhere on everything. Each new entrant needed attention to survive. Each one brought increasingly sophisticated tools to capture it.

But here's the thing nobody talks about: the pie didn't get bigger.

The Fixed Supply Problem

Unlike actual economic resources, human attention cannot be expanded. You cannot work harder to produce more of it. You cannot invest in attention infrastructure. You cannot innovate your way to a 25-hour day.1

The total supply of human attention is fixed at roughly 16 hours per person per day, and that number hasn't changed since the Palaeolithic. If anything, it's shrinking — sleep deprivation is epidemic, attention spans are declining, and the quality of the attention we do have is degraded by constant context-switching.

This means that every new app that enters the market isn't creating new attention — it's stealing it from somewhere else. TikTok's growth didn't come from newly minted attention. It came from YouTube's attention. And Instagram's. And Netflix's. And whatever time you used to spend doing literally anything else.

"In the attention economy, every new product is a predator. There is no empty ecological niche. Every minute one app captures is a minute another app loses."

This is the Ponzi structure. Early platforms captured abundant, unallocated attention and grew explosively. Later platforms can only grow by poaching from earlier ones. And the later you arrive, the harder the poaching becomes, because the existing platforms are also getting better at retention.

The result is an arms race. Each platform invests more in engagement mechanisms — algorithmic feeds, infinite scroll, autoplay, push notifications, variable-ratio reinforcement schedules (the same reward pattern that makes slot machines addictive) — because the only way to grow is to capture attention from competitors, and the only way to capture attention from competitors is to be more addictive than they are.

The Escalation Spiral

Let's trace the escalation.

In 2006, Facebook's News Feed was revolutionary. Just showing you what your friends posted, in reverse chronological order, was enough to capture hours of attention. It was simple. It was effective. It worked because nothing else was doing it.

By 2012, chronological wasn't enough. Facebook introduced the algorithmic feed — content ranked by predicted engagement. This captured more attention per session by showing you the most provocative content first. Other platforms copied the approach.

By 2016, algorithmic feeds weren't enough. The platforms added push notifications, autoplay video, stories (ephemeral content that creates artificial urgency), and live streaming. Each innovation was designed to recapture attention that was leaking to competitors.

By 2020, TikTok blew the entire model apart with an algorithm so aggressive it didn't even need you to follow anyone. It just... knew what you wanted. Or more precisely, it knew what would keep you scrolling. The distinction matters, and we'll come back to it.

By 2025, every platform looks like TikTok. Short-form video everywhere. Infinite algorithmic feeds everywhere. AI-powered recommendation everywhere. The arms race has converged on a single strategy: maximum engagement through maximum algorithmic manipulation of the human dopamine system.

Here's a question that should keep you up at night (if you're not already up at night because your phone buzzed): when every platform is optimising for the same metric — time spent — using the same tools — algorithmic feeds, infinite scroll, push notifications — what exactly is the product they're selling? The answer: you. Specifically, your attention, sold to advertisers in units of time. You are the product. Your attention is the commodity. And the supply is running out.

Why "Time Well Spent" Won't Save Us

Around 2018, a movement called "Time Well Spent" (later rebranded as the "Centre for Humane Technology," co-founded by Tristan Harris, a former Google design ethicist) proposed a different paradigm. Instead of optimising for time spent, platforms should optimise for time well spent — engagement that leaves users feeling good rather than drained.

It's a lovely idea. I genuinely mean that. The diagnosis is correct: platforms are optimising for a metric (engagement time) that diverges from user wellbeing, creating a system where the "best" products are the most addictive, not the most beneficial.

But the prescription — voluntarily adopt better metrics — runs headlong into the incentive structure of the attention economy. A platform that optimises for "time well spent" will, by definition, capture less time per user than a competitor optimising for maximum engagement. In a market where attention is the currency, being less addictive is a competitive disadvantage. It's like asking arms dealers to voluntarily disarm. The ones who do get outcompeted by the ones who don't.

This is the fundamental tragedy of the attention economy: the incentives all point towards more extraction, not less. Every platform knows that engagement maximisation is psychologically harmful. Many of their own researchers have published papers saying so. But the market rewards the most addictive product, so the most addictive product wins, so every product tries to be the most addictive. It's a race to the bottom disguised as a race to the top.

Enter AI: The Accelerant or the Fire Extinguisher?

And now we arrive at the question I've been building towards: what happens when artificial intelligence enters the attention economy? Does it save us or destroy us?

The pessimistic case is straightforward: AI makes the attention extraction machine even more efficient. AI-powered recommendation engines are already better at predicting what you'll engage with than any previous system. AI can generate content — text, images, video — at near-zero marginal cost, flooding the attention market with an infinite supply of perfectly targeted material. AI chatbots create a new category of attention competitor: one that knows you personally, adapts in real time, and never runs out of things to say.2

In this scenario, AI is the ultimate attention Ponzi scheme accelerant. It doesn't create more attention, but it creates infinitely more demand for attention, using increasingly sophisticated tools to extract it. The pie doesn't grow. The number of slicers multiplies.

The optimistic case is more nuanced: AI could act as an attention filter rather than an attention extractor. Imagine an AI assistant that sits between you and the internet, screening everything and only surfacing what's genuinely useful or meaningful. It reads your emails and presents only the ones that matter. It watches your feeds and curates a daily digest of what's actually worth knowing. It acts as a bouncer at the door of your consciousness, turning away the attention pickpockets.

This is technically feasible. The same AI that can predict what will maximise your engagement can also predict what will maximise your wellbeing — those are just different optimisation targets. An AI tuned to protect your attention rather than capture it would be revolutionary.

But — and this is the critical "but" — who builds it? The companies that profit from capturing your attention have no incentive to build tools that protect it. That's like asking a casino to install a machine that tells patrons when they should stop gambling. The incentive structure doesn't support it.

The filter-AI would need to come from somewhere else: a non-profit, a public institution, or a company with a fundamentally different business model. And it would need to be as good at protecting attention as the extraction-AI is at capturing it. That's a tall order.

The Withdrawal Paradox

Here's one more wrinkle that makes this problem truly fiendish: opting out of the attention economy is itself expensive.

Delete social media and you lose touch with friends, miss professional opportunities, and become culturally invisible. Turn off notifications and you miss the important ones along with the unimportant ones. Go off-grid and you discover that modern society is structured around the assumption that you are always reachable, always connected, always available.

The attention economy has made itself load-bearing. It's not a luxury you can strip away without consequences. It's woven into the infrastructure of work, social life, information access, and civic participation. Opting out isn't "going back to a simpler time." It's accepting a genuine competitive disadvantage in a world designed for the permanently connected.

This is what makes it a true Ponzi scheme rather than merely a bad market. In a Ponzi scheme, the late investors can't withdraw without taking losses. In the attention economy, the late participants — which is everyone alive today — can't withdraw without social, professional, and informational costs. We're all invested. We're all locked in. And the returns keep diminishing.

The ultimate irony is that you've spent approximately 12 minutes reading an article about how your attention is being stolen. Those 12 minutes are now gone. They will not be returned. If this article was worthwhile, those minutes were well spent. If it wasn't — if you're now checking your phone because a notification popped up mid-read — then you've just demonstrated the exact problem I'm describing, in real time, and I'm genuinely sorry. But also: please finish the article. I worked hard on it.

Where Does This End?

Ponzi schemes always end. The maths guarantees it. When the inputs can't sustain the outputs, the system collapses.

In the attention economy, I think the "collapse" looks less like a dramatic crash and more like a slow degradation. Attention quality declines as people context-switch more frequently. Engagement becomes shallower as people develop resistance to the manipulation techniques. Trust in platforms erodes as people realise they're being exploited. Young people, who've grown up inside the system, develop coping mechanisms — ironic detachment, aggressive content filtering, "digital minimalism" — that reduce the yield per user.

The platforms respond by escalating further. More immersive formats (AR, VR). More invasive data collection (to better predict engagement). More sophisticated AI (to better manipulate behaviour). Each escalation captures a bit more attention in the short term and degrades the ecosystem further in the long term.

I don't know where the equilibrium is. Maybe there isn't one. Maybe the attention economy is genuinely unsustainable and we're heading for some kind of collective reckoning where society decides that unlimited attention extraction is a public health problem, like smoking or adulterated food.

Or maybe we just keep scrolling. Thinner and thinner slices. Shorter and shorter videos. More and more notifications. Less and less depth. Until one day we look up from our phones and realise we've spent the entire day consuming content about how we should spend less time consuming content.

At which point we'll probably post about it on social media.

For the engagement.
