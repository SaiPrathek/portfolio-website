Every New Year's Resolution Is a Negotiation With a Stranger

MEMO #003 · 02 Jan 2026 · 12 min read

Every New Year's Resolution Is a Negotiation With a Stranger

On temporal discounting, personal identity, and why you're basically doing RLHF on yourself with a 365-day feedback loop.

Psychology Life AI

It's January 2nd. You know what that means. Approximately 40% of the adult population is currently experiencing the brief, intoxicating euphoria of having made a New Year's resolution, paired with the quiet, nagging suspicion that they will not keep it. Both feelings are correct.

I'm not going to give you tips for keeping your resolutions. There are seven billion articles about that and none of them work. What I want to do instead is ask a question that I think is much more interesting: Why do we keep making resolutions that we know — statistically, historically, and in our bones — we won't keep?

The standard answer is "willpower" or "discipline" or "you just need to want it more." This answer is wrong and I'm tired of it. The real answer involves philosophy, neuroscience, behavioural economics, and — I promise this will make sense by the end — the same problem that AI researchers are currently struggling with when they try to align large language models with human values.

Stay with me. This is going to get weird and then it's going to get weirdly useful.

The Person Making the Resolution Is Not the Person Who Has to Keep It

Here is the core problem with New Year's resolutions, stated as plainly as I can manage:

The person who makes the resolution on December 31st is, in every meaningful psychological sense, a different person from the one who has to keep it on February 15th.

I don't mean this poetically. I mean it almost literally.

On December 31st, you are a person at a party (or on a sofa, or at a family dinner), full of champagne and optimism and the narrative weight of a calendar year ending. You are surrounded by cultural cues that say "new beginnings." You are in a heightened emotional state. Your prefrontal cortex is lit up with abstract future-planning, and your limbic system is bathed in the warm glow of social belonging and hope. You are, in that moment, the best version of yourself — the version that sees the long arc of self-improvement and genuinely believes in it.

On February 15th, you are a person who is cold, tired, slightly vitamin D deficient, and being asked to go to the gym at 6:30 AM by a commitment that some stranger at a party made six weeks ago. That stranger had champagne and hope. You have a duvet and an alarm clock that feels like an act of violence. These are not the same person. They share a body and a name, but their motivational landscape, emotional state, and immediate priorities are completely different.

This isn't a character flaw. This is a fundamental feature of how human brains process time, and understanding it changes how you think about pretty much everything.

Temporal Discounting: Why Your Brain Thinks Tomorrow Is Imaginary

Economists and psychologists have a term for the phenomenon: temporal discounting. It refers to the well-documented tendency of humans (and all animals, actually) to value rewards less the further away they are in time. Given the choice between ₹1,000 today and ₹1,500 next week, most people take the ₹1,000 today. The future reward is "discounted" by its distance in time, much like money is discounted by inflation.

This is rational, up to a point. The future is uncertain. A bird in the hand genuinely is worth two in the bush, because the bush might not have any birds when you get there. Our ancestors who prioritised immediate rewards over distant ones survived more often, which is why we all have brains that weight the present so heavily.

But — and this is the crucial part — human temporal discounting is hyperbolic, not exponential. What does that mean? It means we don't discount the future at a steady, consistent rate. We discount it steeply for short time horizons and barely at all for long ones.

The hyperbolic discounting trap: You can genuinely, authentically prefer a healthy future self over your current couch-dwelling self when you think about it in December. But in the moment — at 6:30 AM in February — the discount rate spikes and the immediate reward (warmth, sleep) crushes the distant reward (abs, longevity) despite your "real" preference being the opposite.

This is why resolutions made in December feel so sincere. When you're looking at a goal that's months away, hyperbolic discounting barely affects it. The benefits of exercise, or saving money, or learning a language, all look enormous from a distance. They're barely discounted at all because the sacrifice is also distant.

But when the alarm goes off at 6:30 AM and the sacrifice is right now, the discounting rate goes through the roof. The immediate cost (getting out of a warm bed) is valued at its full, undiscounted face value, while the distant benefit (being fit by summer) is discounted to almost nothing. Your preferences flip — not because you've changed your mind, but because the math your brain is doing has changed.

This is called "preference reversal," and it's one of the most robustly demonstrated phenomena in behavioural economics. You haven't failed. Your brain has done exactly what it's designed to do. Unfortunately, what it's designed to do is optimise for survival on the savannah, not for keeping gym memberships active.

Derek Parfit Would Like a Word

The philosopher Derek Parfit, in his masterwork Reasons and Persons (1984), made an argument so unsettling that philosophy hasn't fully recovered from it: personal identity is not what we think it is.

We walk around assuming that we are a single, continuous self. The "me" who existed ten years ago is the same "me" that exists now and will exist ten years from now. We share a name, a body, memories, an Aadhaar number. Of course we're the same person.

Parfit argued that this is mostly an illusion. What we call "personal identity" is really a chain of overlapping psychological connections — memories, intentions, personality traits, beliefs — and those connections weaken over time. You share a lot of psychological overlap with yourself from last week. You share quite a bit with yourself from last year. But yourself from twenty years ago? You might share a name and some childhood memories, but your values, beliefs, personality, and goals could be almost entirely different.

A New Year's resolution is a contract, but only one party has signed it. The other party doesn't exist yet, and when they do exist, they won't remember agreeing to this.

Parfit's conclusion was radical: we should think of our future selves not as "us" but as other people who we happen to be connected to. People we care about, hopefully, but other people nonetheless. And when you frame it that way, the absurdity of New Year's resolutions becomes blindingly obvious.

Making a resolution is signing a contract on behalf of someone who doesn't exist yet, binding them to a commitment they didn't make, in circumstances you can't predict, and then being disappointed when they don't honour it. It's like hiring someone for a job without interviewing them, giving them no training, and then firing them when they underperform. The failure isn't theirs. It's in the process.

The AI Connection: You're Doing RLHF On Yourself

Now here's where it gets properly interesting. If you've been following AI development at all, you've probably heard of RLHF — Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback. It's the technique that turned base language models (which are impressive but chaotic) into the polished, helpful assistants we use today. The basic idea: you train a model, show humans its outputs, have them rank which outputs are better, and use that feedback to adjust the model's behaviour.

New Year's resolutions are RLHF, except the "human feedback" is coming from a single person (you) who has a massive conflict of interest, the "training loop" runs once a year, and the "model" (also you) keeps getting reset to its base weights every Monday morning.

Let me explain what I mean, because this analogy is disturbingly precise.

In RLHF, you need three things: a reward model (what counts as "good" behaviour), a training signal (feedback that tells the model when it's being good or bad), and frequent iteration (many cycles of feedback and adjustment).

New Year's resolutions fail on all three counts.

The reward model is wrong. When you make a resolution, you're defining "good" behaviour based on December-you's preferences. But February-you has different preferences. It's like training an AI with a reward function that keeps changing — the model can't converge because the target won't stay still. In AI alignment research, this is called "reward hacking" or "specification gaming," and it's one of the hardest problems in the field. Your brain specification-games your resolutions constantly. "I said I'd exercise more. Walking to the fridge counts as exercise. Resolution kept."

The training signal is too sparse. RLHF works because the model gets feedback on thousands of outputs. You get feedback on your resolution approximately never. There's no mechanism that tells you, in real-time, whether you're on track. The "feedback" is looking at yourself in the mirror in July and thinking "huh, those abs still aren't there." By which point you've already lost six months of potential adjustment. It's like training a language model by showing it one example per year and expecting it to learn.

There's no iteration. Good RLHF requires hundreds of training cycles. Resolutions give you one shot per year. If it doesn't work — and it statistically won't — you have to wait twelve months to try again. And when you do try again, you'll make essentially the same resolution with essentially the same strategy, because there's been no structured learning from the failure. It's the definition of insanity, repackaged as tradition.

What Actually Works (Hint: It's Not More Willpower)

So if resolutions are fundamentally broken by temporal discounting, identity drift, and a catastrophically bad training loop — what actually works?

The answer, annoyingly, comes from both behavioural science and AI alignment research, and it converges on the same set of principles:

1. Shorten the feedback loop. Don't set annual goals. Set weekly ones. Weigh yourself every morning, not every January. Track your spending daily, not in a yearly budget review. The shorter the loop between action and feedback, the faster you learn, the faster you adjust. In RLHF terms: increase the training frequency. One cycle per year is useless. Fifty-two cycles per year starts to look like actual learning.

2. Design for February-you, not December-you. When you make a plan, imagine the worst, most tired, most unmotivated version of yourself — because that's the person who has to execute it. Make the plan so easy that even that person can do it. "Go to the gym every day at 5am" is a plan for December-you. "Put on gym shoes when you wake up" is a plan for February-you. Lower the bar until it's embarrassing. Then keep it there until the habit forms.

3. Use commitment devices. A commitment device is anything that makes it harder for your future self to defect. Telling a friend about your goal (social stakes). Paying for a class in advance (financial stakes). Putting your phone in another room at 10pm (environmental design). These work because they add immediate consequences to actions that would otherwise have only distant ones — and immediate consequences are the only language your limbic system speaks.

"The best resolution isn't the most ambitious one. It's the one that's designed to survive contact with the person you'll be in six weeks — tired, busy, and wondering why past-you thought this was a good idea."

4. Treat your future self as a real (different) person. This is the Parfit move, and it's more practical than it sounds. Instead of assuming you'll be motivated, assume you'll be resistant. Write a letter to February-you. Leave notes. Set up systems. Prepare meals in advance. Essentially, be a good friend to the stranger who's going to inherit your commitments — because that's exactly what they are.

The Resolution Resolution

Here's my actual recommendation for the new year, and it's boring in the way that things that actually work tend to be boring:

Don't make a resolution. Make a system. A resolution is a single, brittle commitment that snaps the first time life gets hard. A system is a set of structures — feedback loops, commitment devices, environmental design, shortened iteration cycles — that makes the desired behaviour the path of least resistance.

Resolutions ask: "What do I want to achieve?" Systems ask: "What is the smallest thing I can do repeatedly that will compound into something meaningful?"3

The champagne version of you made a promise. The February version of you needs a framework. Stop negotiating with a stranger and start building infrastructure.

And if the resolution fails anyway — which, statistically, it will — don't wait until next December to try again. That's a 365-day feedback loop, and as we've established, those don't converge. Try again on Monday. Monday is the new January 1st. It comes every week. The training cycle is shorter. The learning is faster. The future self you're designing for is only five days away, which means you might actually remember what they need.

Happy New Year, you magnificent temporal strangers.
