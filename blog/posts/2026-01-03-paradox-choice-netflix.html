The Paradox of Choice But For Netflix (Why You Spend 45 Minutes Choosing a 90-Minute Movie)

More options should mean more satisfaction. Instead, they mean more paralysis, more regret, and more time staring at thumbnails.

Psychology Netflix AI

Last night, I sat down on the sofa at 8:15pm with the firm intention of watching a film. At 9:02pm, I was still scrolling. At 9:15pm, I put on an episode of a show I'd already seen three times. At 9:47pm, I fell asleep. The film was never watched. The evening was, by any reasonable metric, wasted.

This is not a personal failing. This is a systems problem. And it affects approximately every human being with a streaming subscription, which at this point is most of them.

Let me explain why the greatest entertainment delivery system ever created has made us worse at being entertained.

The Blockbuster Advantage (Yes, Really)

In 2004, your average Blockbuster Video had about 6,500 titles. That sounds like a lot, but it's roughly 1% of what Netflix currently offers. On a Friday night, you'd walk in, scan the shelves for fifteen minutes, pick something that looked reasonable, and go home. The movie might be mediocre. But you watched it. And often you enjoyed it, partly because you hadn't spent forty-five minutes agonising over whether something better existed.

Barry Schwartz wrote about this in The Paradox of Choice back in 2004, and the core insight has aged beautifully: more options don't make us happier. They make us more anxious, more paralysed, and more likely to regret whatever we eventually choose.

The mechanism is straightforward. When you have 6 options, you compare them, pick the best one, and feel good. When you have 6,000 options, you can't possibly compare them all, so you're haunted by the ghost of the film you didn't pick. The one three rows down that might have been life-changing. The documentary your friend mentioned that you can't quite remember the name of. The thing that the algorithm suggested last Tuesday that you scrolled past and can now never find again.

"The more options you have, the higher your expectations. And the higher your expectations, the more likely you are to be disappointed by whatever you eventually choose. This is mathematically guaranteed to make you miserable."

The Jam Experiment (And Why It Matters More Than You Think)

In 2000, a Columbia University psychologist named Sheena Iyengar ran one of the most cited experiments in behavioural science. She set up a tasting booth at an upscale grocery store in Menlo Park, California. On some days, the booth displayed 24 varieties of jam. On other days, just 6.

The results were striking, and slightly devastating for anyone in the business of offering people things.

The large display attracted more people — about 60% of shoppers stopped to look, compared with 40% for the small display. More choice was more eye-catching. No surprises there. But here's the bit that matters: of those who stopped at the 24-jam booth, only 3% actually bought a jar. At the 6-jam booth? 30% bought. Ten times the conversion rate. Fewer options, dramatically more action.

Let that sink in for a moment. People were literally ten times more likely to commit to a decision when the field of options was manageable. Not twice as likely. Not three times. Ten.

The 24-jam shoppers did what Netflix users do every single evening: they browsed, they considered, they weighed up options, and then they walked away empty-handed. The cognitive load of comparing two dozen near-identical options wasn't just unhelpful — it was actively paralysing. The shoppers wanted jam. They just couldn't bring themselves to pick one when twenty-three alternatives were staring them in the face, each one a road not taken.

Now, map this onto streaming. Netflix has roughly 15,000 titles globally. Amazon Prime has over 25,000. Even if you narrow it down to "films released in the last five years that are rated above 7 on IMDb and available in your region," you're still looking at hundreds of options on any given platform. You are, in effect, standing in front of a jam display that stretches to the horizon.

And the evidence suggests this is exactly why you can't pick anything.

What's particularly interesting is that the streaming services that have figured this out are the ones with less content, not more. MUBI, the arthouse platform, offers a rotating selection of around 30 films at any time. The Criterion Channel curates themed collections with clear editorial voice. These platforms have significantly higher user satisfaction scores per title watched. Their subscribers don't just watch more — they enjoy more of what they watch. Because when someone has picked the 30 films for you and you trust their taste, the paralysis evaporates. You're not choosing between 15,000 options. You're choosing between 30 options that a human being with actual taste has already vetted.

MUBI's entire value proposition is, essentially, "we've already done the hard bit for you." And it turns out the hard bit was never accessing the content. The hard bit was always the choosing.

"Iyengar's jam study isn't about jam. It's about the fundamental architecture of human decision-making: we are spectacularly bad at choosing when the option set exceeds our cognitive bandwidth. Which, for most decisions, is somewhere around five to seven things."

Maximisers vs Satisficers (Or: Two Types of People at a Restaurant)

Schwartz identified two personality types that handle choice differently. Maximisers want the best possible option. They compare exhaustively, research obsessively, and are never quite sure they made the right call. Satisficers want something that's "good enough." They pick the first option that meets their criteria and move on with their lives.

Satisficers are, across virtually every study, happier. Not because they get better outcomes — maximisers often do objectively better — but because they don't spend the rest of the evening wondering whether the other thing would have been superior.

Applied to Netflix, this maps perfectly:

The Maximiser: Opens Netflix. Scrolls through "Trending." Checks "New Releases." Googles "best films on Netflix January 2026." Reads three reviews. Goes back to Netflix. Can't find the film from the review. Scrolls more. Notices it's been 40 minutes. Feels a creeping sense of despair. Puts on The Office (the show, not the theme).1

The Satisficer: Opens Netflix. Sees a film with an interesting thumbnail. Clicks play. Watches it. Enjoys it. Goes to bed. Wakes up feeling fine.

The satisficer's life is not objectively better. But their experience of their life is, and that turns out to matter more than most people think.

Why the Algorithm Can't Save You

Here's where it gets interesting from an AI perspective. Netflix's recommendation algorithm is one of the most sophisticated machine learning systems on earth. It analyses your viewing history, your ratings, the time of day you watch, how long you hover over thumbnails, what you abandon after ten minutes, and what you rewatch. It serves you personalised rows, personalised thumbnails, even personalised descriptions.

And yet. You still can't pick a film.

"Netflix's algorithm is optimised for what you'll click on, not what you'll enjoy. These are different things, and the gap between them is where your evening goes to die."

This is the fundamental misalignment. The algorithm's reward function is engagement: clicks, play-starts, time-on-platform. Your actual goal is satisfaction: "I want to feel like that was a good use of two hours." These overlap, but not as much as you'd think.

Think about it: the content you click on most readily tends to be familiar, comfortable, and low-risk. True-crime documentaries. Sitcom reruns. The fourth season of something you used to like. The content that would actually maximise your satisfaction might be the weird Icelandic film you'd never click on voluntarily but would genuinely love if someone sat you down and made you watch it.

The algorithm is essentially doing what your Monkey Brain does: optimising for what feels immediately appealing rather than what would actually be good for you. It's the digital equivalent of choosing crisps over the salad. The crisps win every time, not because they're better, but because "better" operates on a timescale the reward function can't see.

The Thumbnail Problem (Or: You're Not Even Seeing the Same Netflix)

Here's something most people don't fully appreciate: the thumbnail you see for a given film on Netflix is not the same thumbnail your friend sees. Netflix runs continuous, large-scale A/B tests on artwork. For any single title, they might have a dozen different thumbnail images in rotation, each tested against different audience segments to determine which version drives the most clicks.

Think about what this means. If you're a user who tends to watch romantic comedies, the thumbnail for Good Will Hunting might prominently feature Matt Damon and Minnie Driver in an intimate scene. If you tend to watch action films, the same movie might show Matt Damon looking intense and brooding. If you watch a lot of Robin Williams content, guess whose face fills the frame. The film hasn't changed. The packaging has been reverse-engineered to exploit your specific behavioural profile.

Netflix's VP of Product once described this as "finding the best artwork to help each member discover content they'll enjoy." Which is a lovely way of framing what is, in practice, manipulation of choice architecture at industrial scale. The image you see for a film is not a representation of that film. It is a carefully optimised piece of advertising, tuned to your psychological profile, designed to maximise the probability that you click. Whether the film behind the thumbnail matches the expectations set by the thumbnail is, from the algorithm's perspective, a secondary concern.

This creates a subtle but insidious problem. You're not browsing a catalogue of films. You're browsing a catalogue of personalised advertisements for films, each one calibrated to appeal to the version of you that the algorithm has constructed from your data. The thing you think you're choosing between — the actual films — is obscured behind a layer of optimised imagery that may have very little to do with the content itself.

It's like going to a restaurant where the menu descriptions change depending on who's reading them. The maximiser in you is already struggling to choose between 15,000 options. Now add the fact that those 15,000 options are being actively misrepresented to make each one look as appealing as possible to you, specifically. The result isn't better choices. It's a deeper, more confused form of paralysis, because even your instinctive thumbnail-based judgements — "that looks good" — can't be trusted. The thumbnails are lying to you. Beautifully, algorithmically, at scale.

The Decision Fatigue Tax

There's another layer to this that most people don't consider: the act of choosing itself is exhausting. Every decision you make, no matter how small, depletes a finite cognitive resource. This is what psychologists call decision fatigue, and it's the reason Barack Obama wore the same suit every day and Steve Jobs wore the same turtleneck.2

By the time you've spent 45 minutes scrolling through Netflix, you haven't just wasted time — you've burned through decision-making capacity that you could have used for literally anything else. You're now too tired to pick a film and too tired to do anything productive instead. The scrolling has consumed you.

This is the hidden cost of infinite choice. It's not just that you can't pick; it's that the process of not picking drains you. The browse itself is the product, and the product leaves you worse off than when you started.

What Would Actually Fix This

The fix is conceptually simple but commercially difficult: optimise the recommendation algorithm for post-watching satisfaction rather than click-through rate.

Imagine a Netflix that, instead of asking "what will this person click on?", asked "what will this person rate 5 stars after watching?" These are different questions with different answers. The first leads to a feed of comfortable, familiar, instantly appealing content. The second might lead to harder sells — films that require a leap of faith, that don't have obvious thumbnails, that take twenty minutes to get going — but that ultimately deliver more value.

Spotify actually does a version of this with Discover Weekly. It doesn't just serve you more of what you already listen to; it serves you things you haven't heard that it predicts you'll love. And it works remarkably well. The difference is that a song costs you three minutes of commitment. A film costs two hours. The stakes are different, and higher stakes make the maximiser in all of us more paranoid.

The Real Solution Is Dumber Than You Think

Here's the truth: the best solution to the Netflix paradox isn't better AI. It's less choice. Curated lists. A "just play something good" button that actually works. A friend who says "watch this, trust me" and you do, because the social trust shortcut bypasses all the anxiety machinery.

We had this, once. It was called "whatever's on TV tonight." It was called "the only film showing at the cinema at 7:30." It was called the constraints of physical reality limiting our options to a manageable number and thereby making the decision for us.

The jam principle shows up everywhere once you start looking for it. Restaurants with shorter menus consistently receive higher satisfaction ratings than those with twelve-page laminated novels. It's not that the food is better. It's that patrons spend less cognitive energy on the ordering process, arrive at their meal in a better mental state, and don't spend the entire dinner wondering whether the other thing would have been superior. The best restaurants in the world rarely offer more than a handful of choices. Some offer none: you eat what the chef has prepared, and you're grateful for the constraint.

The capsule wardrobe movement is the same insight applied to clothing. People who reduce their wardrobe to 30-odd versatile pieces report not just spending less money, but experiencing genuine cognitive relief every morning. The decision of what to wear goes from a daily micro-crisis to a non-event. The constraint isn't a limitation — it's a liberation.

And if you're old enough to remember having three television channels, you'll recall something peculiar: you watched more television, and you enjoyed it more. Not because the programmes were objectively superior (they categorically were not), but because the decision cost was essentially zero. You turned on the telly, you saw what was on, and you watched it or you didn't. There was no paralysis, no scrolling, no ghost of the un-chosen option haunting your evening. The constraint did the choosing for you, and your brain was free to actually enjoy the thing.

This is the counterintuitive truth at the heart of all of this: constraints don't reduce satisfaction. They enable it. The absence of choice is not deprivation. It is, for a brain drowning in options, a form of mercy.

The paradox of choice isn't that choice is bad. It's that unlimited choice, in the absence of trusted curation, creates a decision environment that the human brain was never designed for. We evolved to choose between "the berries on the left bush" and "the berries on the right bush." We did not evolve to choose between 15,000 films while lying on a sofa in our pyjamas.

So next Friday, here's my prescription. Don't open Netflix. Text a friend: "What should I watch?" Watch the first thing they say. Don't look up the rating. Don't read the synopsis. Just press play.

You'll enjoy it more than whatever the algorithm would have served you. Not because the film is better, but because you skipped the forty-five minutes of corrosive indecision that was going to ruin your evening anyway.

The best entertainment system isn't the one with the most content. It's the one that gets out of the way fastest.
