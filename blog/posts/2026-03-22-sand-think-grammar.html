We Taught Sand to Think and Now It's Correcting Our Grammar

We Taught Sand to Think and Now It's Correcting Our Grammar

The absurd journey from silicon to ChatGPT, told as the universe's most unlikely supply chain story.

AI History Computing

Right now, as you read this sentence, billions of tiny electrical signals are racing through a flattened piece of rock at nearly the speed of light so that a pattern of light on a glass screen can rearrange itself into shapes that your brain interprets as language. That rock was, not very long ago, sand on a beach.

Let that settle for a moment. Sand. The stuff between your toes on holiday. The thing children make castles out of. The thing that gets into places you didn't know you had. We took that, did some extraordinarily clever things to it over the span of about eighty years, and now it corrects our grammar. It writes poetry. It passes the bar exam. It argues with people on the internet about whether a hot dog is a sandwich.

This is, objectively, the most absurd supply chain in the history of civilisation, and I think it deserves to be told properly.

Chapter 1: In the Beginning, There Was Sand

Silicon dioxide. SiO₂. The second most abundant element in the Earth's crust, beaten only by oxygen, which is technically cheating because oxygen is part of silicon dioxide anyway. You can't walk ten metres on this planet without stepping on the stuff. Beaches, deserts, riverbeds, the bottom of the ocean — it's everywhere. It's so common that for most of human history, nobody gave it a second thought.

Sand was for glass-making. Sand was for construction. Sand was for getting into your packed lunch at the seaside. That was the full extent of sand's career prospects for roughly the first 200,000 years of human existence.

And then, in the 20th century, we realised something extraordinary. Silicon — the element lurking inside all that sand — is a semiconductor. It doesn't conduct electricity like a metal, and it doesn't block it like rubber. It sits awkwardly in the middle, like someone at a party who hasn't decided whether they're staying or leaving. And that awkward middle ground, that neither-here-nor-there quality, turned out to be the single most important material property in human history.

Because you can control it. You can dope silicon with tiny amounts of other elements — phosphorus, boron — and change whether electricity flows through it. On or off. Yes or no. One or zero.

But I'm getting ahead of myself. Before silicon could change the world, someone had to figure out what to do with a controllable electrical switch. And for that, we need to go to New Jersey.

Chapter 2: Three Men in a Lab (1947)

Murray Hill, New Jersey. Bell Telephone Laboratories. December 1947. Three physicists — William Shockley, John Bardeen, and Walter Brattain — build the first working transistor. It's a messy little thing. Two gold contacts pressed into a sliver of germanium (they hadn't moved to silicon yet). It looks like something you'd find in a junk drawer.

It does one thing: it amplifies electrical signals. That's it. That's the whole trick. You put a small signal in, you get a bigger signal out. Bell Labs wanted it because they were a telephone company and they needed better amplifiers for long-distance calls. The transistor was invented so that when your aunt rang from three states away, you could actually hear her complaining about her neighbour's dog.

Nobody — absolutely nobody — looked at this thing and said, "Ah yes, one day 70 billion of these will fit on a chip the size of a fingernail and collectively hallucinate a convincing essay about the French Revolution."

Here's what's remarkable about the transistor: it replaced the vacuum tube, which did roughly the same job but was the size of a light bulb, generated tremendous heat, and burned out constantly. The entire history of computing is basically the story of making switches smaller. That's it. Smaller switches. Everything else — the internet, artificial intelligence, your phone knowing that you meant "duck" — is a downstream consequence of making switches smaller.

Shockley, for his part, went on to become a deeply unpleasant person who spent his later years promoting eugenics. Bardeen became the only person in history to win two Nobel Prizes in Physics.1 Brattain largely retired to his ranch. The transistor they built went on to become the most manufactured object in human history. More transistors have been produced than any other thing humans have ever made. More than grains of rice. More than bricks. More than regrettable text messages, though that's a closer race than you'd think.

Chapter 3: The Leap to Integration

A transistor by itself is just a switch. Useful, but limited. The magic happens when you combine them. Connect two transistors in a particular way and you get a logic gate — a tiny circuit that can perform basic logical operations. AND, OR, NOT. "Is this true AND that true?" "Is this true OR that true?" "Is this NOT true?"

That's it. That's the entire foundation of digital computing. Every calculation your computer has ever done, every pixel it has ever rendered, every email it has ever sent, every AI model it has ever run — all of it reduces to billions of tiny questions being asked and answered at ludicrous speed. Is this on or off? Yes or no? One or zero?

But in the early days, connecting transistors was a nightmare. You had to wire each one individually by hand. The more complex the circuit, the more wires, the more connections, the more things that could go wrong. This was called the "tyranny of numbers" and it was a genuine engineering crisis. Computers were getting bigger and more complex, but every additional transistor added was another point of failure.

Two men solved this problem independently, almost simultaneously, in one of those convergences that makes you think the universe was trying to tell us something.

Jack Kilby, at Texas Instruments, in 1958: built the first integrated circuit by putting multiple transistors on a single piece of germanium. It looked like a piece of abstract art made by someone having a difficult afternoon.

Robert Noyce, at Fairchild Semiconductor, a few months later: did the same thing but better, using silicon and a process that could actually be manufactured at scale. Noyce went on to co-found Intel. Kilby won the Nobel Prize. Both of them had, without quite realising it, made the modern world possible.

The integrated circuit meant you could put hundreds, then thousands, then millions of transistors on a single chip. And in 1965, Gordon Moore — Noyce's colleague at Fairchild — noticed something odd: the number of transistors you could fit on a chip seemed to double roughly every two years. He wrote it down. It became Moore's Law, which isn't a law at all but rather a self-fulfilling prophecy that the semiconductor industry treated as a deadline for the next sixty years.

Chapter 4: We Gave the Sand Instructions

In 1971, Intel released the 4004 — the first commercially available microprocessor. It had 2,300 transistors, ran at 740 kilohertz, and was designed for a Japanese calculator. A calculator. We built the ancestor of every computer, phone, and AI system on the planet because someone needed a better way to do sums.

But a processor is useless without instructions, and writing instructions in raw binary — strings of ones and zeros — is about as pleasant as writing a novel by flicking a light switch on and off. Someone needed to build a bridge between human thinking and machine thinking.

Enter Grace Hopper. Rear Admiral Grace Murray Hopper, USN. In 1952, she wrote the first compiler — a programme that translated human-readable code into machine language. Before Hopper, if you wanted a computer to do something, you spoke its language. After Hopper, the computer learned to understand yours.

This was a conceptual revolution that most people still underappreciate. Hopper's compiler was the moment we stopped going to the machine and the machine started coming to us. Every programming language that followed — FORTRAN, C, Python, JavaScript, all of them — is a descendant of that insight: humans shouldn't have to think like machines. We should make machines think more like humans.

If that sounds like a straight line from compiler to ChatGPT, that's because it basically is. The entire history of software is the story of raising the level of abstraction, one layer at a time, until the interface between human and machine is just... language. Plain language. "Write me an email." "Summarise this document." "Actually, I think you meant 'whom'."

"The history of computing is the history of humans being too lazy to do things the hard way. Every breakthrough was essentially someone saying 'there has to be a less annoying way to do this.' And then finding it."

Chapter 5: The Brains That Weren't (1958–1986)

Now here's where the story gets properly weird, because running parallel to all the hardware progress was a completely separate group of people asking a much stranger question: can we make the sand think?

In 1958, Frank Rosenblatt at Cornell built the Perceptron — a machine that could learn to recognise simple patterns. It was, in the most generous interpretation, a brain made of wires. It had a single layer of artificial neurons that could be trained to classify inputs by adjusting the strength of their connections. Feed it enough examples and it would learn, sort of, in the way that a dog learns which cupboard the treats are in.

The New York Times, in a fit of journalistic enthusiasm that has since become a tradition in AI coverage, reported that the Perceptron was "the embryo of an electronic computer that [the Navy] expects will be able to walk, talk, see, write, reproduce itself and be conscious of its existence."

It could sort cards into two categories.

But the hype was real, and when the Perceptron turned out to have severe mathematical limitations — Marvin Minsky and Seymour Papert published a devastating critique in 1969 — the backlash was equally real. Funding dried up. Researchers moved on. Neural networks became an academic backwater. This was the first AI Winter, and it lasted most of the 1970s.

Then it happened again. In the early 1980s, a second wave of AI enthusiasm — this time around expert systems, which were basically very fancy if-then statements — generated enormous investment, enormous promises, and enormous disappointment when the systems turned out to be brittle, expensive, and not particularly intelligent. By 1987, the second AI Winter had arrived. Neural networks were, by this point, about as fashionable as flared trousers.

And yet. In the background, stubbornly, a handful of researchers kept working. Chief among them: Geoffrey Hinton.

Chapter 6: Backpropagation, or How the Sand Learned From Its Mistakes

In 1986, David Rumelhart, Geoffrey Hinton, and Ronald Williams published a paper that would, eventually, change everything. The paper described backpropagation — a mathematical technique for training neural networks with multiple layers.2

The idea is deceptively simple. You show the network an input. It produces an output. You compare the output to what the answer should be. Then you work backwards through the network, adjusting each connection by a tiny amount to make the output slightly less wrong. Repeat this millions of times and the network gets good at whatever you're training it on.

This is, if you squint, how learning works. You try something, you get it wrong, you adjust, you try again. The difference is that a neural network can do this millions of times per second, across millions of examples, without getting bored, frustrated, or distracted by its phone. It has, in that narrow sense, infinite patience.

But in 1986, there was a problem. Multiple layers of neurons required enormous amounts of computation, and the computers of 1986 weren't up to the task. Backpropagation was a brilliant idea imprisoned in hardware that couldn't execute it. It was like having the blueprint for a jet engine in 1803 — theoretically sound, practically useless. Hinton spent the next twenty-six years waiting for the hardware to catch up.

This is one of the great underappreciated dynamics of the computing revolution: the software ideas often arrive decades before the hardware can run them. The maths for neural networks existed in the 1980s. The silicon needed to make them practical didn't arrive until the 2010s. The gap between knowing what to do and being able to do it was bridged entirely by making the switches smaller.

Chapter 7: The Cambrian Explosion (2012–2017)

In 2012, something happened that made the entire AI research community sit up very suddenly. Geoffrey Hinton's group at the University of Toronto entered the ImageNet challenge — an annual competition where algorithms try to correctly classify images — and obliterated the competition. Their deep neural network, AlexNet, reduced the error rate by a margin so large that it didn't look like an improvement. It looked like a different sport.

The secret ingredient was threefold: more data (ImageNet had millions of labelled images), more compute (they trained on GPUs, graphics cards originally designed for video games), and deeper networks (more layers, made possible by backpropagation and brute computational force).

Overnight, deep learning went from academic curiosity to the hottest field in technology. Every major tech company scrambled to hire anyone who knew what a gradient was. Hinton, the man who'd spent three decades in the wilderness believing neural networks would work if only the computers were powerful enough, was vindicated in spectacular fashion.

What followed was a cascade. Deep learning conquered image recognition, then speech recognition, then machine translation, then game-playing (DeepMind's AlphaGo beat the world champion at Go in 2016, a game so complex that brute-force search was considered impossible). Each success fed more investment, which funded more research, which produced more breakthroughs.

And then, in June 2017, a team at Google published a paper with the most understated title in the history of paradigm shifts: "Attention Is All You Need."

Chapter 8: Attention Is All You Need (and Also a Lot of GPUs)

Ashish Vaswani and seven co-authors — working on, of all things, machine translation — proposed a new architecture called the Transformer. The key innovation was the "attention mechanism," which allowed the model to look at all parts of an input simultaneously, rather than processing it sequentially word by word. Instead of reading a sentence from left to right like a human, the Transformer could consider every word's relationship to every other word all at once.

This sounds like a technical detail. It was not a technical detail. It was the architectural breakthrough that made large language models possible. Everything that followed — BERT, GPT-2, GPT-3, GPT-4, Claude, Gemini, all of it — is built on the Transformer.

Vaswani et al. were trying to improve Google Translate. They ended up providing the blueprint for machines that can write screenplays, debug code, explain quantum physics to a five-year-old, and, yes, politely inform you that the grammatically correct form is "to whom it may concern."

I need to pause here and stress how insane the supply chain has become at this point.

Chapter 9: The Most Unlikely Supply Chain in History

Let me now lay out the full absurdity of what we've accomplished, stated plainly.

We dig sand out of the ground. We heat it to 1,700 degrees Celsius to extract the silicon. We purify that silicon to 99.9999999% purity — that's nine nines, making it one of the purest materials humans have ever produced. We grow it into a single crystal ingot using a process that involves slowly rotating a seed crystal in a vat of molten silicon for days. We slice that ingot into wafers using diamond-tipped saws. We coat the wafers in light-sensitive chemicals and shine ultraviolet light through intricately patterned masks to etch circuits smaller than a virus. We stack these circuits into three-dimensional architectures containing billions of transistors, each one switching on and off billions of times per second.

Then we write software. Layer upon layer of abstraction — machine code, assembly, operating systems, high-level languages, frameworks, libraries — until the instructions bear no resemblance whatsoever to the ones and zeros being executed underneath. We build neural networks — mathematical structures inspired by (but not actually much like) biological brains — and train them on essentially the entire written output of human civilisation.

And the end result is a system that looks at your email and says: "I notice you wrote 'less meetings.' Did you mean 'fewer meetings'? 'Less' is used for uncountable nouns, while 'fewer' is used for countable nouns."3

Sand is correcting your grammar. And it's right. That's the worst part. It's right.

Chapter 10: Nobody Planned This

What makes this story truly extraordinary is that none of it was planned. Not one person, at any point in this eighty-year chain, understood what they were ultimately building towards.

Shockley, Bardeen, and Brattain were trying to build a better telephone amplifier. Kilby and Noyce were trying to solve a wiring problem. The Intel engineers behind the 4004 were building a calculator chip. Grace Hopper wanted programmers to stop wasting time writing binary. Frank Rosenblatt thought he was building a pattern recogniser for the Navy. Hinton believed neural networks would work but couldn't prove it for thirty years. Vaswani and his co-authors were trying to improve Google Translate.

Each step was individually rational. Each person was solving the problem immediately in front of them. But the cumulative result — the totality of what all these individual rational steps produced when chained together — is something that no one predicted, no one planned, and, honestly, no one would have believed if you'd described it in advance.

"We're going to take sand from a beach, purify it until it's almost atomically perfect, carve microscopic circuits into it using concentrated light, stack ten billion switches on a surface the size of a postage stamp, then teach it to write poetry."

You'd have been sectioned.

And yet here we are. The human species, through no grand plan, no central coordination, no five-year roadmap, stumbled — brilliantly, chaotically, improbably — from a messy transistor in a New Jersey lab to a system that can hold a conversation about Kierkegaard. The most important invention in human history was built by people who, for the most part, had no idea they were building it.

The Cathedral and the Beach

I want to end on something that I think gets lost in the breathless discourse about AI — the fear, the hype, the hot takes, the predictions of doom or utopia.

What humans have done with sand is, by any measure, the most remarkable achievement of our species. More remarkable than the pyramids, more remarkable than the moon landing, more remarkable than penicillin or the printing press or the internal combustion engine. Because those were all, in some sense, understandable extensions of existing capabilities. Humans build things with stone. Humans explore. Humans mix chemicals. The conceptual leap from "sand on a beach" to "a machine that understands language" is so vast, so unfathomably enormous, that it makes everything else we've accomplished look like a warm-up exercise.

And we did it collaboratively. Not in the way that a corporation collaborates, with org charts and quarterly targets, but in the deepest possible sense: generations of human beings, across decades and continents, each contributing their piece without seeing the whole. Shockley enabling Kilby enabling Moore enabling Hopper enabling Rosenblatt enabling Hinton enabling Vaswani enabling... whatever comes next. An unbroken chain of curiosity, stubbornness, and occasionally questionable career decisions, stretching from a beach to a thought.

"Every person who ever picked up sand and wondered what it was made of was, without knowing it, taking the first step toward a machine that would one day correct their grammar."

The next time you use an AI — to write an email, to summarise a document, to check whether it's "who" or "whom" — take a moment to consider the chain of events that made that possible. The molten silicon. The diamond saws. The photolithography masks. The billions of transistors switching billions of times per second. The decades of mathematical theory. The AI winters endured by researchers who couldn't prove they were right. The six million parameters. The eight hundred million parameters. The one point seven trillion parameters.

All of it — every ounce of ingenuity, every late night in a lab, every rejected grant proposal, every career gambled on an unfashionable idea — converging on this moment. You, reading a sentence. A machine, somewhere, ready to tell you that you've dangled a participle.

We taught sand to think.

It took us eighty years, roughly a thousand Nobel-calibre insights, and an amount of electricity that would make a climate scientist weep. But we did it. Not because anyone set out to do it. But because humans, when left to their own devices, cannot stop tinkering. Cannot stop asking "what if." Cannot stop picking up the stuff beneath their feet and wondering what it could become.

That impulse — that restless, stubborn, magnificent refusal to leave things as they are — is the most human thing about us. And the machine that now corrects your grammar? It's a monument to it. The greatest monument we've ever built. Made of sand.
