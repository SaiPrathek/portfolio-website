Love Is Just Two Reward Functions That Accidentally Aligned

A Valentine's Day special. Oxytocin, dopamine, and the alignment problem — in relationships and in AI. Why falling in love is basically a reward function hijack.

RelationshipsAINeuroscience

Happy Valentine's Day. Or, if you prefer a more neuroscientifically precise greeting: Happy Oxytocin-Mediated Pair-Bonding Reinforcement Day. I know. It doesn't fit on a card.

Here's what's actually happening in your brain when you "fall in love." Your ventral tegmental area is flooding your nucleus accumbens with dopamine. Your hypothalamus is pumping out oxytocin. Your serotonin levels have dropped to levels clinically similar to obsessive-compulsive disorder (this is not a metaphor — a 1999 study by Marazziti et al. literally found that the serotonin transporter levels of people in the early stages of romantic love were indistinguishable from those of OCD patients). Your prefrontal cortex — the part responsible for rational judgement — is being actively suppressed.

In other words: you are not thinking clearly. You are, neurochemically, in approximately the same state as someone with a clinical disorder that compels them to check whether they've locked the door seventeen times. Except instead of the door, it's your phone. And instead of checking the lock, you're checking whether they've texted back yet.

Romance!

The Three-Chemical Cocktail

Anthropologist Helen Fisher, who has spent decades scanning the brains of people in love, identifies three distinct neurochemical systems involved in human pair-bonding, each with its own reward function:

1. Lust (testosterone + oestrogen). The crudest system. Its objective function is simple: reproduce. It doesn't care with whom. It doesn't care about compatibility, shared values, or whether this person will still be interesting over breakfast. It is the brute-force search algorithm of human mating — low specificity, high recall. "Is this person alive and approximately the right species? Good enough. Proceed."

2. Attraction (dopamine + norepinephrine + serotonin). This is the "falling in love" system. Its objective function is more targeted: focus on this specific person. The dopamine creates craving. The norepinephrine creates alertness and energy (this is why you can't sleep when you're newly in love). The serotonin drop creates obsessive thinking. Together, they produce a state that Fisher compares to addiction — and she's not being metaphorical. Brain scans of people in early romantic love show activation patterns in the reward system that are essentially identical to those of cocaine users.

3. Attachment (oxytocin + vasopressin). The long-game system. Its objective function is: stay together. This system activates during physical intimacy, shared experiences, and caregiving. It's what keeps couples together after the dopamine-fuelled insanity of early attraction fades. It's calmer, steadier, and considerably less exciting. It's the difference between a rocket launch and a satellite orbit — the launch is thrilling but unsustainable; the orbit is what actually gets the job done.

Notice the "Handoff Zone" — that period roughly 6-18 months into a relationship where the insane dopamine-driven attraction starts to fade and the calmer oxytocin-driven attachment is supposed to take over. This is where an enormous number of relationships fail. The dopamine is wearing off, the person suddenly seems less magical, their annoying habits are no longer charming quirks, and the whole thing starts to feel like a mistake.

It isn't a mistake. It's a phase transition. But people who don't understand the neurochemistry interpret the loss of dopamine-driven excitement as evidence that they chose the wrong person, when really it's just evidence that the attraction system completed its job and handed off to the attachment system. If you bolt every time the dopamine fades, you'll spend your life in an endless series of 18-month relationships, forever chasing a neurochemical state that was designed to be temporary.1

The Alignment Problem (In Love)

Here's where the AI parallel gets interesting. In artificial intelligence, the alignment problem is the challenge of ensuring that an AI system's objective function (what it's optimising for) actually matches what we want it to do. The classic example: you tell an AI to maximise paperclip production. If it's sufficiently capable and insufficiently aligned, it turns the entire planet into paperclips. The objective function was specified, but not what we actually meant.

Relationships have the same problem. Your brain's attraction system has a very specific objective function: it optimises for novelty + physical attractiveness + status + arousal. These are the signals that trigger dopamine release. These are the features your reward function is actually measuring when you "fall for someone."

But what you think you're optimising for — what you'd say if asked — is compatibility, shared values, emotional maturity, kindness, and long-term potential. This is your stated objective. It's the objective you'd write in the documentation.

The problem is that your stated objective and your actual reward function are badly misaligned. You say you want someone kind and emotionally available. Your dopamine system rewards you for someone exciting and physically attractive. When these two things conflict — and they often do — the reward function usually wins. This is why people keep dating people who are wrong for them. It's not that they're stupid. It's that their reward function is optimising for the wrong thing, and it's running at a lower level than conscious decision-making.

In AI alignment terms: your prefrontal cortex wrote a perfectly sensible objective function ("find a compatible long-term partner"). But the actual training signal comes from your limbic system ("DOPAMINE NOW"), and the limbic system doesn't care about your long-term objectives. Your limbic system is an AI with a badly specified reward function, and it has root access to your emotions.

This is, I think, the deepest reason why dating is so confusing. You're not a single agent making rational decisions. You're a multi-agent system where different agents have different objective functions that frequently conflict. The lust system wants physical gratification. The attraction system wants novelty and excitement. The attachment system wants stability and safety. Your prefrontal cortex wants long-term compatibility. And they're all running simultaneously, with different time horizons, different discount rates, and different definitions of "success."

Falling in love isn't finding "the one." It's finding someone whose reward function happens to align with yours well enough that you can tolerate the misalignments. That's less romantic than the fairy tale version. It's also more accurate, more useful, and — if you think about it — more impressive.

The Arranged Marriage Puzzle

Here's a finding that makes Western romantics deeply uncomfortable: multiple studies have found that people in arranged marriages report comparable or higher levels of long-term satisfaction compared to people in love marriages.

A well-cited 2012 study by Epstein, Pandit, and Thakar found that love in arranged marriages tends to gradually increase over time, while love in love marriages tends to gradually decrease. After about ten years, the two groups converge. After twenty years, the arranged marriage group often reports slightly higher satisfaction.

How is this possible? Through the lens of reward functions, it actually makes perfect sense.

In a love marriage, the initial selection is driven primarily by the attraction system — dopamine, novelty, physical chemistry. These features have very low predictive power for long-term compatibility. You're selecting based on a reward signal that will be largely irrelevant in five years. It's like hiring a software engineer based on their handshake — technically a signal, but not a very good one for the job they'll actually be doing.

In an arranged marriage (at least in its non-coercive, modern form), the selection is driven by family evaluation of compatibility factors: similar values, education, socioeconomic background, family harmony, temperament complementarity. These features have much higher predictive power for long-term satisfaction. The initial dopamine hit is smaller (or absent), but the attachment system has better raw material to work with.

In AI terms: love marriages are selected by a reward function with high variance and low bias (exciting but unreliable). Arranged marriages are selected by a reward function with low variance and high bias (boring but predictable). Over time, the low-variance selection tends to win — the same way that in machine learning, a less exciting but more reliable model often outperforms a flashy one in production.

I'm not arguing for arranged marriages. I'm arguing that the process of "falling in love" is a terrible selection mechanism for long-term partnership, and that acknowledging this might save people a lot of heartbreak. You can have the dopamine rush and use your prefrontal cortex. You just have to be aware that they're optimising for different things, and deliberately weight the prefrontal cortex's input higher than your limbic system would naturally prefer.

Multi-Agent Alignment: The Relationship Version

In multi-agent AI systems, alignment is even harder than in single-agent systems. When you have two AI agents that need to cooperate, you need to align not just each agent with human values, but each agent with the other agent. Their reward functions need to be compatible enough to sustain cooperation.

A long-term relationship is a multi-agent alignment problem. Two agents, each with their own reward functions, need to cooperate indefinitely. The question isn't whether your reward functions are identical — they never will be. The question is whether they're compatible enough to be negotiated.

The couples who survive long-term are the ones who explicitly negotiate a shared objective function. "We want these things. We value these things. When our individual preferences conflict, here's how we'll resolve it." This sounds unromantic. It is unromantic. It also works.2

John Gottman, the psychologist who can predict divorce with 94% accuracy after watching a couple argue for fifteen minutes, found that the biggest predictor of relationship failure isn't conflict itself — it's the presence of what he calls "the Four Horsemen": contempt, criticism, defensiveness, and stonewalling. In alignment terms, these are all symptoms of the same thing: the two agents' reward functions have diverged to the point where cooperation is no longer mutually beneficial. Contempt, in particular, is the signal that one agent has stopped modelling the other as a cooperative partner and started modelling them as an adversary. Once that happens, the alignment is broken in a way that's very difficult to repair.

What This Means (Practically)

I realise that reframing love as "two reward functions that accidentally aligned" is not exactly the stuff of greeting cards. But I think there's something genuinely useful here — something that, far from diminishing romance, actually increases your chances of finding lasting love.

Because if you understand the neurochemistry, you stop making a very common mistake: confusing the dopamine signal with the compatibility signal. They feel similar. They're not the same thing. The dopamine says "this person is exciting." The compatibility signal — which is quieter, slower, and comes from the prefrontal cortex rather than the limbic system — says "this person is someone I can build a life with." The best relationships have both. But if you can only have one, the second is the one that lasts.

Similarly, if you understand that the attraction phase is designed to end — it's a rocket booster, not an engine — you stop panicking when it does. You stop interpreting the end of the dopamine phase as the end of the relationship. You recognise it as a transition, not a failure. The attachment system is less exciting than the attraction system, but it's the one you'll actually live in.

And if you understand that you're a multi-agent system — that your lust, attraction, attachment, and rational planning systems all want different things — you can at least be more intentional about which system you listen to. Your limbic system is going to scream. Let it scream. Then ask your prefrontal cortex what it thinks. It's slower, quieter, and right more often.

Happy Valentine's Day. May your reward functions converge, and may the convergence hold.
