Why We're All Running Someone Else's Code

MEMO #014 · 11 Feb 2026 · 10 min read

Why We're All Running Someone Else's Code

Richard Dawkins invented memes in 1976 — the original concept, not the internet kind. Your beliefs, habits, and identity are programs you didn't write. So are an AI's.

MemeticsCultureAI

Here's a thought experiment. Try to name a single belief you hold — about politics, religion, morality, what constitutes good music, whether pineapple belongs on pizza — that you arrived at entirely through independent reasoning, with zero influence from your parents, your friends, your culture, or any media you've ever consumed.

Take your time. I'll wait.

You can't do it. I can't do it. Nobody can do it. Every thought in your head was either directly installed by someone else or assembled from components that were installed by someone else. Your entire mental operating system — your values, your aesthetics, your sense of humour, your political intuitions, your model of what a "successful life" looks like — is, in the most literal sense, code you didn't write running on hardware you didn't choose.

In 1976, a biologist named Richard Dawkins proposed a name for these self-replicating cultural programs. He called them memes. And no, he absolutely did not mean the thing with the cat.

The Original Meme (Not That Kind)

Dawkins coined the term in The Selfish Gene, his landmark book about evolution. His argument was simple but radical: genes aren't the only replicators on Earth. Ideas replicate too. A catchy tune, a religious belief, a fashion trend, a scientific theory, a catchphrase — these are all units of cultural information that spread from brain to brain, evolving as they go.

He derived the word "meme" from the Greek mimeme (meaning "imitated thing"), shortening it to rhyme with "gene." Because genes and memes, Dawkins argued, follow the same evolutionary logic. Both are replicators: they make copies of themselves. Both are subject to variation: copies aren't always perfect. Both face selection: some variants spread more successfully than others. Both can be selfish: a meme can spread not because it benefits its host, but simply because it's good at spreading.

That last point is the unsettling one. A gene doesn't have to be "good for the organism" to spread — it just has to be good at getting itself into the next generation. Similarly, a meme doesn't have to be true or useful to spread. It just has to be good at getting itself copied from one brain to another.

A meme doesn't care whether it's true. It doesn't care whether it's useful. It cares — to the extent that a pattern of information can "care" about anything — only about whether it gets copied. Truth is nice, but virality is better.

This reframing is powerful because it lets you look at cultural phenomena from the meme's perspective rather than the human's perspective. Why does the flat Earth theory persist despite being trivially disprovable? Because the meme has features that make it exceptionally good at replicating: it's simple, it's contrarian (which makes the believer feel special), it has a built-in immune system ("that's what they want you to think"), and it creates strong in-group identity. The meme doesn't need to be true. It just needs those viral features.

How Memes Infect You

Let's trace the actual infection pathway. How does a meme get from one brain to another?

Notice a few things about this diagram. First, the infection doesn't happen in a vacuum — every new meme is evaluated against the host's existing memeplex (the interconnected web of memes already installed). This is why converting someone's political views is so difficult. You're not just replacing one meme; you're asking them to uninstall an entire operating system of interconnected beliefs, social identities, and emotional commitments. It's like telling someone to switch from macOS to Linux: technically possible, emotionally traumatic, and they'll spend the first six months angrily googling how to do basic things.

Second, notice that memes get modified as they're transmitted. This is the "variation" part of the evolutionary analogy. The game of Telephone isn't a bug — it's a feature. Every time a meme passes through a brain, it gets slightly altered to fit that brain's existing beliefs, emotional needs, and communication style. This is how urban legends evolve, how religious doctrines drift over centuries, and how a story about your slightly embarrassing night out becomes an epic saga of debauchery by the fourth retelling.1

The Major Codebases

If memes are individual programs, then what are the big ideological systems — religion, political philosophy, cultural traditions — ? They're codebases. Entire operating systems, built from thousands of interconnected memes, maintained by communities of developers (priests, politicians, professors, influencers), and running on millions of human brains simultaneously.

Let's look at some of the most successful codebases in human history:

Religion. Perhaps the most successful memeplex ever created. Consider the features that make major religions so effective at spreading: a compelling origin story (creation myths), an answer to existential anxiety (afterlife), a community enforcement mechanism (congregations), built-in transmission instructions ("go forth and spread the word"), an immune system against competing memes ("thou shalt have no other gods"), regular reinforcement rituals (weekly services), and emotional hooks (love, fear, hope, guilt). A Silicon Valley growth hacker would weep with envy at this feature set. Religions are, memetically speaking, the most optimised software ever deployed on human hardware.

Political ideology. Left vs. right, progressive vs. conservative — these aren't just different policy preferences. They're different operating systems that process the same inputs (current events, social problems) and produce systematically different outputs (proposed solutions). Jonathan Haidt's moral foundations research shows that liberals and conservatives literally weight different moral values differently: liberals prioritise care and fairness; conservatives additionally weight loyalty, authority, and sanctity. Same hardware, different software, different outputs.

"Hustle culture." A more recent but spectacularly viral memeplex. Core memes include: "sleep is for the weak," "grind now, rest later," "your network is your net worth," "I don't have hobbies — I have income streams." Each meme reinforces the others, creating a self-consistent worldview that, critically, is very easy to signal on social media (which is the 2020s equivalent of having built-in transmission instructions). Hustle culture is, memetically, a very well-designed virus — it's simple, it makes the host feel superior, it's visually shareable, and it has a built-in immune response to criticism ("you're just jealous/lazy").

Fashion and aesthetic trends. Why does everyone suddenly want the same kitchen aesthetic? The same font on their website? The same style of furniture? Because aesthetic preferences are memes too, and social media has turned aesthetic transmission from a slow drip into a firehose. The "clean girl aesthetic" wasn't independently invented by ten million women — it was a meme that replicated through TikTok at a speed that would have been physically impossible before algorithmic content distribution.

Here's what makes memetics uncomfortable: most people believe their worldview is the result of rational, independent thought. Memetics suggests it's mostly the result of which memes happened to reach them first, which ones were compatible with their existing memeplex, and which transmission vectors they were exposed to. You didn't choose your beliefs any more than you chose your native language. You just caught them.

The AI Training Data Parallel

And now we arrive at the connection that's been nagging at me since I first read Dawkins twenty years ago: large language models are the most transparent example of memetic inheritance ever created.

Think about what an LLM actually is. It's a system that has ingested billions of words of human-generated text — books, articles, forum posts, social media, Wikipedia, code repositories, legal documents, love letters, hate speech, scientific papers, conspiracy theories, recipes, and everything in between. Then it's learnt statistical patterns in that text and uses those patterns to generate new text.

In other words: it's running on inherited code it didn't choose. Just like us.

The parallels are almost too clean. When an LLM generates a biased or toxic output, we say "the training data was biased." When a human expresses a prejudiced view, we should, by the same logic, say "the cultural training data was biased." In both cases, the system is faithfully reproducing patterns from its inputs. Neither the model nor the person independently invented the bias — they inherited it.

This is why AI alignment is such a deep problem. You can't just train a model on "good" data, because the definition of "good" is itself a meme — a culturally transmitted, historically contingent, constantly evolving concept. Whose version of "good"? The 2020s Western liberal version? The 1950s version? The version from a culture with entirely different moral foundations? The training data is always someone's code, reflecting someone's memeplex.

And this is equally true for humans. The version of morality you're running — whatever it is — was installed by a specific culture at a specific time. If you'd been born in a different century, a different country, a different family, you'd be running different moral software. You'd hold different beliefs with equal conviction. You'd think your worldview was the result of independent rational thought. You'd be just as wrong.

What Makes a Meme Go Viral?

Not all memes spread equally. Some ideas catch fire; others die immediately. What determines which ones win? Research in cultural evolution and social psychology points to several key features:

Emotional payload. Memes that trigger strong emotions — anger, fear, disgust, awe, humour — spread faster than neutral ones. This is because emotional arousal increases the likelihood of sharing. A study by Jonah Berger at Wharton found that content evoking "high-arousal" emotions (anger, anxiety, awe) was shared significantly more than content evoking "low-arousal" emotions (sadness) or no emotion at all. This is why outrage spreads faster than nuance. The algorithm doesn't prefer outrage because it's evil; it prefers outrage because we do.2

Simplicity. Complex, nuanced ideas don't spread well. "Taxation is theft" spreads better than a 300-page analysis of optimal tax policy. "Move fast and break things" spreads better than "carefully consider the second and third-order effects of rapid iteration in contexts where failure costs are asymmetrically distributed." Memes are compressed precisely because compression aids transmission. The simpler the idea, the more accurately it copies.

Identity attachment. The most persistent memes are ones that become part of the host's identity. Once a belief shifts from "something I think" to "something I am," it becomes nearly impossible to dislodge. "I think electric cars are a good idea" is a belief. "I'm a Tesla person" is an identity. The identity version will survive contradictory evidence; the belief version might not. This is why political polarisation is so intractable — people have made their politics their identity, and asking someone to change their identity is asking them to die a small death.

Social proof. We copy what others are doing. If a meme appears popular, it becomes more popular. This creates runaway positive feedback loops. It also explains why trends feel like they come from nowhere: you see one person doing something, then three, then suddenly everyone is doing it and pretending they always have been. The meme's popularity is the meme's evidence of quality. This is circular. Nobody cares.

The Uncomfortable Implication

If all of this is true — if our beliefs, values, and identity are largely composed of inherited memes that we didn't choose, selected not for truth but for viral fitness — then what does "thinking for yourself" even mean?

I think it means something more modest and more difficult than we usually imagine. You can't escape memetic inheritance any more than you can escape genetic inheritance. You will always be running on inherited code. But you can do something most biological organisms can't: you can inspect the code.

You can look at a belief you hold and ask: "Where did this come from? Did I arrive at this through evidence and reasoning, or did I absorb it from my environment? Is this belief serving me, or am I serving it? Would I hold this belief if I'd been born in a different time, a different place, a different family?"

Most of the time, the honest answer to that last question is "probably not." And that's okay. It doesn't mean the belief is wrong — it might still be true. But it means you should hold it with appropriate humility. Your operating system is a product of your environment, and so is everyone else's.

You can't uninstall the operating system while you're running on it. But you can at least open Task Manager and see what processes are active. That's the beginning of something like intellectual freedom — not the absence of inherited code, but the awareness of it.

The same applies to AI. We can't train a model on "no data" — it needs training data the way a human needs a culture to grow up in. But we can be more intentional about what goes into the training set, more careful about what biases we're encoding, more transparent about the choices we're making. Not because we can achieve perfect neutrality — we can't, any more than a human can — but because awareness of the inherited code is the first step towards choosing which parts to keep and which parts to refactor.

You're running someone else's code. So am I. So is every AI system ever built. The question isn't whether you can avoid it. The question is whether you know it's happening.

And now you do. Which means from this point forward, every belief you hold without examining is a choice. Not a passive inheritance — an active decision to keep running code you haven't reviewed.

Good luck with the code review. It's a big codebase. I'd recommend starting with the most emotionally charged parts — those are the ones most likely to contain bugs.
