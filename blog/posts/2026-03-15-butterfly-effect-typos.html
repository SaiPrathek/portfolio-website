The Butterfly Effect But For Typos

How a missing hyphen destroyed a rocket, a wrong unit killed a Mars probe, and a software flag cost $440 million in 45 minutes.

Software Chaos History

On 22 July 1962, at Cape Canaveral, a rocket called Mariner 1 lifted off the launch pad, veered dramatically off course, and was blown up by the range safety officer 293 seconds into flight. The investigation that followed uncovered one of the most expensive punctuation errors in human history. A single missing overbar — which you can think of as a hyphen, or a tiny horizontal line above a letter in a handwritten formula — had been omitted when the guidance equations were transcribed into code. The rocket, faithfully executing its flawed instructions, did exactly what it was told. It just wasn't told the right thing.

Cost: $18.5 million in 1962 dollars. Adjusted for inflation, about $185 million today. For a hyphen.

This is a story about how the smallest possible mistakes create the largest possible consequences. It is also, if you squint, a story about everything that is terrifying about modern software development. But let's not get ahead of ourselves. First, let's talk about rockets.

Mariner 1: The Most Expensive Hyphen in History

The Mariner programme was NASA's first attempt to send spacecraft to other planets. Mariner 1 was bound for Venus. It was carrying instruments to measure radiation, magnetic fields, and cosmic dust. It was, by the standards of 1962, an extraordinary achievement of engineering. Hundreds of scientists and engineers had spent years building it. The launch itself went perfectly. The Atlas-Agena rocket performed beautifully. And then, about five minutes in, the guidance system started doing something unusual.

The spacecraft began to veer left. Then it veered more. The range safety officer, watching the telemetry, realised that Mariner 1 was heading towards the North Atlantic shipping lanes. He pressed the destruct button. The rocket exploded. Venus would have to wait.

The post-mortem revealed that a guidance equation had been transcribed from handwritten notes into the computer software, and during that transcription, an overbar — a symbol indicating that a particular value should be smoothed, or averaged over time — was dropped. Without the smoothing, the guidance system was responding to raw, noisy radar data. Every tiny fluctuation in the tracking signal caused a correction. Those corrections compounded. The rocket started chasing its own noise.

Arthur C. Clarke later called it "the most expensive hyphen in history." Technically it was an overbar, not a hyphen, but the point stands: a mark smaller than your little fingernail, omitted from a single equation, turned a $18.5 million spacecraft into an extremely expensive firework.

Think about this the next time you dismiss a code review comment about a missing semicolon. The universe does not grade on a curve. It does not care that your mistake was small. It cares what your mistake does.

Mars Climate Orbiter: When Imperial Met Metric (And Nobody Checked)

If Mariner 1 was a tragedy, the Mars Climate Orbiter was a farce. It is the story of a $327 million spacecraft that was destroyed because one team used pounds and another team used newtons and nobody, at any point in the process, said "hang on, are we all using the same units?"

It was 1999. NASA had contracted Lockheed Martin to build the spacecraft's thruster software. Lockheed's software produced thrust data in pound-force seconds — an imperial unit. NASA's navigation team, receiving that data, assumed it was in newton-seconds — the metric equivalent. One pound-force is about 4.45 newtons. So every thrust calculation was off by a factor of roughly 4.45. Not a huge factor, in the grand scheme of things. Barely noticeable on any individual manoeuvre.

But the Mars Climate Orbiter was on a nine-and-a-half-month journey. Over that time, the small errors accumulated. Each trajectory correction was slightly wrong. Each course adjustment pushed the spacecraft a little further from where it was supposed to be. By the time it arrived at Mars, it was 170 kilometres closer to the planet than intended. Instead of entering a stable orbit, it dipped into the upper atmosphere. Mars, indifferent to human unit conventions, tore it apart.

$327 million. Nine and a half months of flight. Destroyed because two teams were speaking different measurement languages and nobody thought to ask whether they were on the same page. The investigation report is one of the most politely devastating documents in aerospace history. It essentially says: "We had a column that said 'units' and nobody filled it in."

"Complex systems don't fail because of big, dramatic, obvious errors. They fail because of small, boring, invisible ones that nobody thought to check — because who checks the obvious things?"

Knight Capital: How to Lose $440 Million Before Lunch

Now let's leave space and come back to Earth. Specifically, to the trading floor of Knight Capital Group on the morning of 1 August 2012.

Knight Capital was one of the largest market makers in the United States. They handled roughly 11% of all US equity trading volume. They were a serious, well-established firm. They had risk management. They had procedures. They had, in theory, all the safeguards you'd want.

What they also had was a deployment process that was about to go catastrophically wrong.

Here's what happened. Knight was preparing to participate in a new NYSE programme called the Retail Liquidity Programme. This required updating their trading software, called SMARS. The update involved deploying new code to eight servers. A technician deployed the new code to seven of them. The eighth server was missed. It still had the old code.

Now, here's where it gets properly nightmarish. The old code on that eighth server contained a function that had been repurposed. Years earlier, there had been a feature flag — a software toggle called "Power Peg" — that controlled a specific type of trading behaviour. The Power Peg functionality had been retired, but the flag itself had been reused in the new code to activate something completely different. On the seven correctly updated servers, the flag meant "participate in the Retail Liquidity Programme." On the eighth server, running the old code, the flag still meant "execute Power Peg" — an aggressive, high-volume trading strategy that was never meant to be activated.

When the market opened at 9:30am, the eighth server started executing Power Peg trades at extraordinary speed. It was buying high and selling low, across 154 different stocks, as fast as the exchange could process orders. Knight's risk management systems flagged unusual activity, but by the time humans understood what was happening and pulled the plug, it was 10:15am. Forty-five minutes. In that time, Knight Capital had accumulated $7 billion in unwanted positions and lost approximately $440 million.

The company, which had been worth $365 million the day before, was effectively bankrupt. It was acquired in a fire sale six days later.

Let me restate the cause: one server out of eight wasn't updated. A repurposed software flag on that server triggered legacy behaviour. Forty-five minutes. $440 million. Game over.

Therac-25: When Bugs Kill People

The stories so far have been about money. Expensive, embarrassing, but ultimately recoverable. Nobody died when Mariner 1 exploded. Nobody was on the Mars Climate Orbiter. Knight Capital's shareholders lost a fortune, but shareholders have, historically, survived losing fortunes.

The Therac-25 is a different kind of story.

The Therac-25 was a radiation therapy machine used in hospitals in the 1980s. It could deliver two types of treatment: a low-power electron beam (for surface tumours) and a high-powered X-ray beam (for deep tumours). Previous models — the Therac-6 and Therac-20 — had hardware interlocks: physical mechanisms that made it impossible to deliver the high-powered beam without the proper safety configuration. The Therac-25 replaced those hardware interlocks with software controls. It was cheaper. It was more flexible. It was also, as it turned out, running code with a race condition.

A race condition is what happens when two processes share a resource and the outcome depends on which one gets there first. In the Therac-25, if an operator typed commands quickly enough — specifically, if they changed the treatment type and then quickly moved to the next field within a window of about eight seconds — the software could end up in a state where the machine was configured for high-powered X-ray mode but believed it was in low-power electron mode. The safety checks passed. The beam fired. But instead of a therapeutic dose, the patient received a blast of radiation roughly 100 times the intended amount.

Between 1985 and 1987, at least six patients were massively overdosed. Three of them died. The injuries were horrific: patients described feeling a sensation like an "electric shock" or "intense heat" during treatment. One patient's arm had to be amputated. The manufacturer, AECL, initially insisted the machine was safe and that the reported incidents were impossible. It took multiple independent investigations to uncover the bug.

The root cause was a one-byte counter overflow in the software. When a specific variable rolled over from 255 to 0 — which happened approximately once every 256 treatment setups — the safety interlock was bypassed. That's it. One byte. One variable. One overflow. Three people dead.1

Why Modern Software Makes This Exponentially Worse

Those stories span from 1962 to 2012. You might think that fifty years of progress would have made these kinds of failures less likely. You would be wrong. Modern software has made them more likely, more frequent, and potentially more devastating. Here's why.

A typical modern application doesn't just contain the code you wrote. It contains hundreds or thousands of dependencies — libraries, frameworks, and packages written by other people, often maintained by a single volunteer in their spare time. Your beautiful web application, the one that processes financial transactions or controls medical equipment, is sitting on top of a tower of code that you've never read, written by people you've never met, maintained with varying degrees of enthusiasm.

In March 2016, a developer named Azer Koçulu unpublished a package called left-pad from npm, the JavaScript package registry. left-pad was eleven lines of code. All it did was pad a string with spaces or zeros on the left side. It was, by any measure, trivial. It was also a dependency of thousands of other packages, which were dependencies of thousands more, which were dependencies of applications used by millions of people. When left-pad disappeared, builds broke across the entire JavaScript ecosystem. React, Babel, and countless other major projects couldn't install their dependencies. Thousands of deployment pipelines failed simultaneously.

Eleven lines of code. Written by one person. Removed in a moment of frustration. And for a few hours, a significant chunk of the internet's development infrastructure ground to a halt.

This is the dependency problem, and it's getting worse. The average Node.js project now has over 1,000 transitive dependencies. Each one is a potential point of failure. Each one is maintained by someone who might get bored, or angry, or hacked. The xz utils backdoor discovered in 2024 showed how a patient attacker could spend years building trust as an open-source maintainer before inserting malicious code into a library used by virtually every Linux distribution on earth. That's not a typo — that's deliberate — but the attack surface is the same: deep dependency chains that nobody fully understands.

Charles Perrow, a sociologist at Yale, wrote a book in 1984 called Normal Accidents. His thesis was simple and unsettling: in complex, tightly coupled systems, catastrophic failures aren't anomalies. They're inevitable. He called them "normal" not because they're acceptable, but because they're a natural, predictable consequence of how the system is built. When components are tightly coupled (A depends on B depends on C depends on D) and the interactions are complex (the output of one component is the input of another in ways the designers didn't anticipate), then failures will cascade in ways nobody predicted. It's not a question of if. It's a question of when.

Perrow was writing about nuclear power plants and chemical factories. He could just as easily have been writing about npm.

The Uncomfortable Truth About Testing

After each of these disasters, the post-mortem reports all say some version of the same thing: "This should have been caught in testing." And they're right. It should have been. The missing overbar should have been caught in code review. The unit mismatch should have been caught in integration testing. The unpatched server should have been caught in deployment verification. The race condition should have been caught in safety analysis.

But here's the thing: testing catches the bugs you think to test for. It does not, and cannot, catch the bugs you don't think to test for. The Mariner 1 team tested their guidance software extensively. They just didn't test the specific scenario where one symbol was omitted from one equation. NASA tested the Mars Climate Orbiter's navigation thoroughly. They just didn't test the scenario where one contractor was using different units. Knight Capital tested their deployment process. They just didn't verify that all eight servers received the update. AECL tested the Therac-25's software. They just didn't test the scenario where an operator typed faster than the software expected.

Every one of these failures existed in the gap between what was tested and what was possible. And that gap is, in any sufficiently complex system, infinitely large. You can test a million scenarios and the failure will come from scenario one million and one. This is not a failure of diligence. It is a mathematical certainty. The space of possible states in a complex system is so vast that exhaustive testing is not just impractical — it is physically impossible. You'd need more time than the age of the universe to test every possible state of a moderately complex programme.2

Enter AI: Saviour or Accelerant?

This is the point in the essay where I'm supposed to say "and that's where AI comes in to save us." And there is a version of that story that's true. AI-powered code analysis tools can scan millions of lines of code and flag potential issues that humans would miss. They can detect unit mismatches, identify race conditions, spot deployment inconsistencies, and highlight dependency vulnerabilities. GitHub Copilot and its successors can suggest safer patterns and catch obvious mistakes in real time. Static analysis tools powered by machine learning are getting genuinely good at finding the kinds of subtle bugs that have historically caused disasters.

But there's a darker version of this story, and I think it's more important.

AI code generation tools are now writing enormous quantities of code, very fast, with very little friction. A developer using Copilot or Claude or GPT can generate hundreds of lines of code in minutes. That code is usually mostly correct. It compiles. It passes basic tests. It looks reasonable. But "mostly correct" is exactly the danger zone where catastrophic bugs live. The bugs that destroy rockets and kill patients aren't the ones that cause compilation errors. They're the ones that work perfectly 999 times out of 1,000 and then, on the 1,001st execution, do something nobody expected.

When a human writes code slowly, they build a mental model of what the code does. They understand the edge cases because they thought through them while writing. When an AI generates code quickly, the human reviewing it may not build that mental model. They see code that looks correct, passes tests, and appears to work. They ship it. And somewhere in that code, there might be a race condition, or a unit assumption, or an off-by-one error that won't manifest until production, under load, on a Tuesday, when the counter variable rolls over from 255 to 0.

AI doesn't eliminate the butterfly effect. It potentially scales it. Instead of one human making one typo in one equation, you now have millions of developers shipping AI-generated code with subtle assumptions that nobody fully understands, into systems with thousands of dependencies that nobody fully audits, running on infrastructure that nobody fully controls.

The bugs won't be bigger. They'll be more numerous. And in a world of complex, tightly coupled systems, more small bugs means more cascading failures. Perrow's "normal accidents" don't just apply to nuclear plants and space missions anymore. They apply to everything that runs on software, which is, at this point, everything.

What We Can Actually Do About This

I don't want to end on pure doom, so here are some things that actually help, drawn from the actual post-mortems of actual disasters:

Redundancy of verification, not just redundancy of systems. Knight Capital had redundant servers. The problem wasn't that they lacked backup systems; it was that nobody verified the deployment across all of them. The Mars Climate Orbiter had multiple teams checking navigation. None of them checked that the units matched. Having two people review code is useless if both people make the same assumptions.

Kill the "it's too obvious to check" instinct. The most dangerous bugs are the ones that seem too trivial to worry about. Missing hyphens. Unit labels. Deployment checklists. The more obvious something seems, the less likely anyone is to verify it, which is precisely why it's the thing that goes wrong. The best engineering cultures are the ones where checking the obvious things isn't seen as paranoid — it's seen as professional.

Loose coupling over tight coupling. When systems are loosely coupled, failures stay local. When they're tightly coupled, failures cascade. The reason left-pad took down the JavaScript ecosystem wasn't that left-pad was important — it was that the dependency chain was so tight that removing one eleven-line package caused a cascade through thousands of others. Design systems where one broken component doesn't bring down everything else.

Respect the gap between "it works" and "it's correct." Code that compiles is not code that's correct. Tests that pass are not proof of correctness. A deployment that succeeds is not a deployment that's safe. The gap between "appears to work" and "actually works in all cases" is where every single one of these disasters lived. Treat that gap with the respect it deserves.

And finally, when you're reviewing code at 4pm on a Friday and you see something that looks fine but feels slightly off — a variable name that doesn't quite match, a unit that isn't explicitly labelled, a flag whose purpose isn't immediately clear — don't dismiss the feeling. Don't tell yourself it's probably fine. Don't assume someone else checked it.

Ask the question. Check the units. Verify the deployment. Read the equation one more time.

Because the butterfly effect doesn't care that your mistake was small. It only cares what happens next.
