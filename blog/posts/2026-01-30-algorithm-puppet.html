The Algorithm Doesn't Know You. It Knows a Puppet That Looks Like You.

On the divergence between who you are and who the algorithm thinks you are â€” and why the puppet is slowly winning.

AI Privacy Philosophy

Meet Your Digital Doppelganger

Somewhere inside the servers of every major technology company, there is a version of you. It has your name. It has your face (or at least, a hash of your device ID that serves the same purpose). It has a detailed record of everything you've ever clicked, watched, liked, hovered over, scrolled past, or lingered on for more than three seconds. It is, according to the systems that built it, you.

It is not you.

It is a puppet. A statistical marionette constructed from your behavioural exhaust â€” the digital trail of clicks and scrolls you leave behind like footprints in snow. And here's the deeply weird part: this puppet is now making decisions on your behalf. It decides what you see when you open YouTube. It decides what appears in your Instagram feed. It decides which news articles are surfaced and which are buried. It decides, in a very real sense, what information enters your brain on any given day.

And the puppet is wrong about you. Not completely wrong â€” it's eerily right about some things. But it's wrong in ways that matter, and the wrongness is getting worse, and almost nobody is paying attention because the puppet is just good enough to be convincing.

How the Puppet Gets Built

Let me explain how recommendation algorithms actually work, because the popular understanding ("the algorithm tracks you!") is technically correct but conceptually misleading. It makes it sound like there's a filing cabinet somewhere with a folder labelled "Your Name" containing a detailed dossier. The reality is simultaneously less sinister and more unsettling.

A recommendation algorithm doesn't model you as a person. It models you as a point in a high-dimensional vector space. You are a list of numbers. Each number represents a weighted feature â€” how much you watch cooking videos, how often you engage with political content, your tendency to click on thumbnails with red text, your propensity to watch videos all the way through versus abandoning them at the thirty-second mark. You are decomposed into these features, and the resulting vector is your puppet.

The puppet can't capture context. It knows you watched three cooking videos on Tuesday. It doesn't know you were avoiding a work deadline. It knows you clicked on a political article. It doesn't know you regretted it immediately. It knows you spent twenty minutes on a video essay about the decline of the Roman Empire. It doesn't know you fell asleep at minute four and your phone kept playing.

The puppet is a flattened, averaged, context-free summary of your behaviour. It is you with all the nuance removed. It is you as seen by a very diligent but entirely literal-minded stalker who can track your every click but has no theory of mind â€” no ability to infer why you did anything, only that you did it.

The Engagement Trap

Here's where it gets properly concerning. The puppet isn't optimised to show you things you'll enjoy. It's optimised to show you things you'll engage with. And those are very, very different things.

Engagement is not satisfaction. You can engage intensely with something that makes you miserable. Outrage is engaging. Anxiety is engaging. The algorithm doesn't care whether you're happy â€” it cares whether you're clicking.

Think about the last time you fell into a YouTube rabbit hole at midnight. You watched video after video, each one auto-playing into the next, each one keeping you engaged. Were you enjoying yourself? Honestly? Or were you in a kind of content trance â€” not actively choosing to watch, but not actively choosing to stop? When you finally closed the app and looked at the clock, did you feel satisfied? Or did you feel vaguely hollowed out, like you'd eaten an entire tube of Pringles and couldn't remember deciding to do so?1

This is the engagement trap. The algorithm's puppet thinks you love those midnight rabbit holes because you engage with them intensely. High watch time. Low exit rate. The puppet's numbers are excellent. But the puppet doesn't know that you went to bed feeling like you'd wasted two hours, or that you woke up tired, or that you would have preferred to watch one thoughtful twenty-minute documentary and go to sleep. The puppet optimises for what you do, not for what you'd want to have done.

There's a crucial distinction in economics between "revealed preferences" (what you actually choose) and "stated preferences" (what you say you'd choose). Recommendation algorithms are built entirely on revealed preferences â€” your actual behaviour. But revealed preferences are contaminated by impulse, habit, context, and interface design. The fact that you clicked on something doesn't mean you wanted to click on it. It means the thumbnail was compelling, or the title provoked curiosity, or the autoplay feature removed the friction of choosing. Revealed preference, in the context of algorithmically curated feeds, is less "what you prefer" and more "what you're susceptible to."

The Feedback Loop From Hell

The puppet doesn't just observe you. It shapes you. And this is where the metaphor starts to get genuinely dark.

Here's how the feedback loop works. The algorithm shows you content based on your puppet's profile. You engage with some of it (because it was selected to maximise engagement). Your engagement updates the puppet's profile. The updated puppet gets served more of the same, slightly intensified. You engage again. The puppet updates again. And slowly, gradually, imperceptibly, you start to become the puppet.

Your puppet thinks you're interested in political outrage. So it shows you political outrage. You click on it (because outrage is engaging). Your puppet's political_engagement score goes up. More outrage appears. You click again. Eventually you start to think of yourself as someone who cares deeply about politics, when what actually happened is that an algorithm exploited your outrage response and reinforced it until it became a habit.

Your puppet thinks you like true crime podcasts. So it fills your recommendations with true crime. You listen to them because they're there and they're easy. You start to think of yourself as a true crime fan. You weren't, originally. You watched one episode of one series because a friend recommended it. But the puppet grabbed that signal, amplified it, and turned a single data point into a personality trait.

The terrifying question: How many of your "interests" are things you genuinely chose, and how many are things an algorithm chose for you and then reinforced until you forgot there was ever an alternative?

This is the feedback loop from hell: the puppet shapes the feed, the feed shapes your behaviour, your behaviour shapes the puppet, and the cycle continues until the puppet and the person converge. You become a simplified version of yourself â€” the version that's easiest for the algorithm to predict and serve. Your rough edges get sanded down. Your contradictions get resolved (by eliminating one side). Your capacity for surprise gets eroded, because surprise is bad for prediction accuracy.

The Filter Bubble Is a Hall of Mirrors

Eli Pariser coined the term "filter bubble" back in 2011, and in the fifteen years since, the concept has become both widely understood and widely underestimated. Most people think of filter bubbles in political terms â€” "I only see liberal news" or "I only see conservative news." And that's real, but it's the least interesting version of the phenomenon.

The deeper filter bubble is aesthetic, cultural, and intellectual. Your algorithm doesn't just filter your politics. It filters your taste. It narrows the music you hear, the ideas you encounter, the perspectives you consider, and the types of creativity you're exposed to. It creates a hall of mirrors where everything you see is a reflection of what you've already seen, slightly distorted.

I notice this most acutely with music. Spotify's algorithm is, in many ways, brilliant. It's very good at giving me music I'll listen to. But it's terrible at giving me music I haven't heard before â€” genuinely new genres, genuinely unfamiliar artists, things that are outside my established taste profile. My Discover Weekly playlist is, every week, a collection of songs that sound almost exactly like songs I already like. It's efficient and predictable and has completely eliminated the experience of being surprised by music.

I used to discover music by accident. A friend would play something in their car. A song would come on the radio that I'd never have chosen but that caught me off guard. I'd walk into a shop and hear something that didn't fit any of my categories. These accidents were how my taste grew. The algorithm has eliminated the accidents. And in eliminating the accidents, it has eliminated the growth.2

The Puppet Doesn't Know What You Don't Do

There's a specific failure mode of recommendation algorithms that I think about constantly. The algorithm models what you engage with. It cannot model what you would engage with but haven't been exposed to. It has no concept of "things you'd love if you knew they existed."

Imagine there's a podcast out there that you would absolutely adore â€” it's exactly your thing, it would change how you think about a topic you care about, you'd recommend it to everyone you know. But you've never heard of it. And because you've never heard of it, you've never searched for it, never clicked on it, never engaged with it. To the algorithm, it doesn't exist in your preference space. Your puppet has no signal to attach to it.

The algorithm can only recommend things that are similar to things you've already consumed. It interpolates within your existing taste space. It cannot extrapolate beyond it. This means the most transformative recommendations â€” the ones that would genuinely change your mind or expand your horizons â€” are precisely the ones the algorithm is least likely to make.

This is a known problem in recommendation systems, and it has a name: the "cold start" problem (for new content that has no engagement data) and the "exploration-exploitation trade-off" (should the algorithm show you something it knows you'll engage with, or something risky that you might not?). In practice, exploitation almost always wins, because showing you something risky might reduce engagement, and engagement is the metric that matters. The algorithm is a coward. It will always choose the safe bet.

Taking the Puppet's Strings Away

So what do we do? How do you maintain your autonomy when a sophisticated statistical system is gradually reshaping your preferences from the inside?

I don't have a grand solution, but I have some observations that I find useful.

First: cultivate intentional consumption. The algorithm is most powerful when you're passive â€” when you open an app with no specific purpose and let the feed carry you. It's weakest when you arrive with intent. "I'm going to watch this specific documentary" is a fundamentally different mode than "I'll just see what's recommended." The first is you making a choice. The second is the puppet making a choice for you. Deliberately choosing what to consume â€” even sometimes â€” breaks the feedback loop.

Second: introduce randomness. The algorithm hates randomness because randomness is unpredictable and unpredictability reduces engagement metrics. But randomness is how you discover things outside your bubble. Browse a random Wikipedia page. Walk into a bookshop and buy whatever catches your eye. Ask a friend whose taste is completely different from yours for a recommendation. These are the digital equivalent of walking down a street you've never been on â€” you might find nothing, or you might find something that changes everything.

Third: remember that the puppet is not you. This sounds obvious but it isn't. The puppet is a simplified model, and simplified models are wrong by definition. They're useful approximations â€” but an approximation of a person is not a person. When the algorithm shows you something and you catch yourself thinking "I must like this, because it was recommended to me," pause. Ask yourself: do I actually want this? Or has the puppet convinced me that I do?

Fourth: be suspicious of your own preferences. This is the hardest one. If you've been using algorithmic platforms for years â€” and you have, we all have â€” then some percentage of what you think of as "your taste" has been shaped by the feedback loop. Your preference for certain types of content, certain tones, certain aesthetic styles â€” how much of that is genuinely yours, and how much was gradually installed by a system optimising for your engagement? I don't know. You don't know either. And that uncertainty is itself valuable, because it keeps you questioning rather than accepting.

The Puppet Paradox

I want to end on a thought that I find genuinely difficult. The puppet is getting better. As algorithms improve, as data collection expands, as models become more sophisticated, the gap between the puppet and the person shrinks. At some point, conceivably, the puppet becomes a very accurate model of who you are.

But "who you are" at that point has been substantially shaped by the puppet's influence. The model converges on its target, but the target has been moving towards the model the entire time. It's not that the algorithm finally understood you. It's that you and the algorithm met in the middle â€” you became more predictable, and it became more accurate, and the result is a person who is easier to model because they've been gradually simplified.

The puppet paradox is this: the better the algorithm gets at modelling you, the less there is of you to model. Your rough edges, your contradictions, your capacity for surprise â€” these are the things that make you irreducible to a vector of numbers. And they're the things that the algorithm slowly files away, because they're noise in the signal, and the algorithm's job is to eliminate noise.

The goal, I think, is not to escape the algorithm â€” that's impossible in 2026, short of moving to a cabin in the woods and communicating exclusively via hand-written letters. The goal is to maintain a gap between yourself and the puppet. To be, always, slightly more complex than the model. To do things the algorithm doesn't expect. To want things the algorithm can't predict. To be, in the face of sophisticated statistical modelling, stubbornly, irreducibly, delightfully human.

The puppet will keep trying to catch up. Let it try. Just make sure it never quite succeeds.
