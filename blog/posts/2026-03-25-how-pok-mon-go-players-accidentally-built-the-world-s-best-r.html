How Pokémon Go Players Accidentally Built the World's Best Robot Navigation System (And What That Says About Us)

How Pokémon Go Players Accidentally Built the World's Best Robot Navigation System (And What That Says About Us)

For ten years, millions of people walked around catching imaginary creatures. Turns out they were also training the most sophisticated pedestrian navigation dataset ever created. This is a story about games, labour, and the strange ways we accidentally build the future.

AI crowdsourcing gaming machine-learning human-behaviour

Let me tell you about my uncle Derek.

Derek is 58 years old, has a bad knee, and until 2016 hadn't voluntarily walked anywhere since the invention of the car remote starter. Then Pokémon Go came out, and suddenly Derek was walking 8 kilometres a day to catch a digital rat named Pikachu in a park near his house.

"It's not exercise," he told me once, slightly out of breath, phone clutched in his hand like a holy relic. "I'm just... hunting."

Derek thought he was playing a game. Niantic — the company behind Pokémon Go — knew he was doing something else entirely. Something that would take a decade to fully reveal itself.

He was training robots to walk.

The Accidental Army of Cartographers

Here's what happened: for ten years, approximately 500 million people1 downloaded Pokémon Go and proceeded to walk around their neighbourhoods, parks, cities, and shopping centres while their phones quietly recorded everything. The paths they took. The obstacles they avoided. The shortcuts they discovered. The places where footpaths actually exist versus where Google Maps thinks they exist (spoiler: these are often hilariously different).

Every time someone walked around a bollard, their phone noted: "humans don't walk through bollards." Every time someone took a diagonal across a park instead of following the path, their phone noted: "humans are efficient and will absolutely ignore that 'keep off the grass' sign." Every time someone paused at a kerb, waited for traffic, then crossed at a spot with no zebra crossing, their phone noted: "this is where humans actually cross, not where the infrastructure suggests they should."

This data is, quite literally, priceless for anyone trying to build robots that navigate human spaces. Because here's the thing about robot navigation: it's not actually that hard to get a robot to move from point A to point B. The hard part is getting it to do so without terrifying everyone around it.

The Uncanny Valley of Movement

Humans are extraordinarily good at detecting when something is moving "wrong." We evolved this skill because things that move wrong are often things that are injured (potential food) or things that are pretending to be normal while actually being dangerous (potential predator). Our brains have a finely-tuned wrongness detector, and it fires constantly at early-generation robots.

Watch an early Boston Dynamics robot walk sometime. Technically, it's walking. Mechanically, it's perfect. But something in your lizard brain screams: that thing is not okay and I should leave.

The difference between a robot that can navigate and a robot that can navigate like a human is the difference between a cover letter written by ChatGPT and one written by an actual person. Both technically accomplish the task. Only one doesn't make you vaguely uncomfortable.

To move naturally through human spaces, robots need to learn the unwritten rules. The social physics. The fact that you don't walk directly at someone and expect them to move. That you slow down near children. That you give dogs a wider berth than adults. That you don't stop in doorways (a rule many humans have also failed to learn, but I digress).

This kind of knowledge cannot be programmed. It can only be learned. And the best way to learn it is to watch millions of humans doing it, over and over, in every possible context.

Enter: everyone who ever caught a Charizard.

The Beautiful Scam (That Wasn't Really a Scam)

I want to be careful here, because "company tricks users into providing free labour" is a tired narrative that's often more cynical than accurate. Niantic didn't hide what they were doing. They're a mapping company — they spun out of Google, for goodness' sake. The spatial data collection was always part of the deal.

But there's something philosophically interesting about the exchange that happened here.

"People gave away the most detailed pedestrian movement data ever collected, and in exchange, they got... happier?"

Because here's the thing: Pokémon Go genuinely improved a lot of people's lives. Studies showed it increased physical activity. It got people out of the house. It created unexpected social connections. My uncle Derek lost 12 kilograms and made friends with a group of teenagers who taught him about "IV breeding" (I still don't know what this means, and I've chosen not to ask).

So we have a situation where:

• Users got: exercise, fun, social connection, dopamine
• Niantic got: the most valuable pedestrian navigation dataset in human history
• Future robots get: the ability to not be creepy

Is this a fair trade? I genuinely don't know. But it's a fascinating one.

The Deeper Pattern

This isn't actually a story about Pokémon Go. It's a story about a pattern that's becoming increasingly common: the gamification of data collection.

CAPTCHA made us label images to train AI while proving we weren't robots (irony noted). Duolingo uses our translations to build language models. Waze turns our commutes into traffic data. Amazon's recommendation engine learns from every click, every hover, every abandoned cart.

We are all, constantly, training machines. The question is whether we're doing it consciously.

I'm not trying to be alarmist. This isn't a "delete your apps" rant. I think most of these exchanges are genuinely fine, even beneficial. But I do think we should be aware of them.

Because the Pokémon Go revelation isn't that Niantic did something nefarious. It's that they did something clever — so clever that most people didn't even realise they were part of it until a decade later.

What This Means for You (Yes, You)

Three takeaways, because I'm contractually obligated to make this practical:

1. Your behaviour has value beyond the obvious. When you use any app, you're not just consuming a service. You're generating data about how humans behave. This data has downstream applications you cannot predict. This isn't necessarily bad — it's just true.

2. Fun is a powerful motivator for collective action. Niantic couldn't have paid people to walk around collecting this data. It would have been prohibitively expensive and the data would have been worse (paid workers take shortcuts; players trying to catch rare Pokémon are thorough). The lesson: if you want humans to do something at scale, make it genuinely enjoyable. This applies to everything from fitness apps to civic engagement.

3. The future is being built in weird ways. Ten years ago, if you'd told robotics researchers that their biggest breakthrough in pedestrian navigation would come from a game about catching pocket monsters, they would have laughed. But here we are. The future rarely arrives through the front door. It sneaks in through the games we play, the shortcuts we take, the silly apps we download because we're bored on the train.2

The Punchline

I called my uncle Derek while writing this piece. Told him about the robot navigation thing. There was a long pause.

"So you're telling me," he said slowly, "that all those hours I spent walking around in the rain, trying to catch a bloody Snorlax outside Tesco... I was teaching robots to walk?"

"Basically, yes."

Another pause.

"Well," he said finally, "at least the robots will know not to stop in the middle of the pavement. I taught them that. I walked around those people very aggressively."

And honestly? That might be Derek's greatest contribution to the future of artificial intelligence. Somewhere, in a training dataset, there's a data point that says: "When humans encounter other humans who have stopped inexplicably in a walkway, they navigate around them with what can only be described as passive-aggressive velocity."

The robots will learn. The robots will understand.

The robots will be just as annoyed as the rest of us.
