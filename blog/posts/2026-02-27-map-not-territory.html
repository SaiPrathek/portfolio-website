The Map Is Not the Territory But Google Maps Is Getting Dangerously Close

On models, reality, and the strange loop where the map starts reshaping the territory it claims to merely describe.

PhilosophyAIMaps

In 1931, a Polish-American philosopher named Alfred Korzybski stood before the American Association for the Advancement of Science and said something that would echo through the next century of thought about knowledge, models, and reality:

"The map is not the territory."

It's one of those phrases that sounds obvious the moment you hear it but gets more profound the longer you sit with it. Of course a map isn't the territory. A map of Mumbai is not Mumbai. A menu is not a meal. A photograph of your dog is not your dog (though try telling that to your mum, who has 847 photos of the dog and approximately four of you).

But here's what Korzybski was really getting at, and what makes his observation genuinely important rather than merely clever: we forget this all the time. We constantly mistake our models of reality for reality itself. We confuse the simplification with the thing being simplified. And when the model is good enough — when the map is detailed enough, when the abstraction is convincing enough — the confusion becomes invisible.

Which brings us to Google Maps. Because Google Maps is, I would argue, the most influential model of reality ever constructed by human beings. And it's doing something Korzybski never anticipated: it's not just representing the territory. It's changing it.

A Brief History of Being Lost

To understand why Google Maps is philosophically significant, you first need to appreciate just how bad humans are at spatial navigation without tools.

For most of human history, getting from point A to point B required either (a) local knowledge passed down through oral tradition, (b) following a road that someone else had already figured out, or (c) a willingness to be lost for extended periods and possibly die. Options (a) and (b) covered about 95% of all journeys. Option (c) is how we got the Age of Exploration, which was really the Age of Being Lost But Doing It Very Confidently While Wearing a Big Hat.

Paper maps were a massive upgrade. For the first time, you could carry a model of a large geographical area in your pocket. But paper maps had critical limitations: they couldn't tell you where you were on the map, they couldn't update in real time, and they absolutely could not refold themselves after you'd opened them in the car.1

Then, in 2005, Google Maps launched. And the relationship between humans and geography changed permanently.

The Model That Ate Reality

Here is the crucial thing about Google Maps that separates it from every map that came before: it doesn't just describe the territory. It influences behaviour at a scale that reshapes the territory itself.

Consider restaurants. Research has consistently shown that a restaurant's Google Maps rating, its position in search results, and even its location pin accuracy have measurable effects on its revenue. A restaurant that appears prominently on Google Maps gets more foot traffic. A restaurant with a 4.5-star rating outperforms a 4.3-star restaurant by a significant margin, even if the food is identical. A restaurant that Google Maps routes people past — because it's on the "suggested route" to somewhere else — gets more walk-ins than one that's technically closer but off the algorithmic beaten path.

This means that Google Maps is not neutrally describing the restaurant landscape. It's sculpting it. Restaurants succeed or fail partly based on their position in Google's model. The map is changing the territory.

This isn't limited to restaurants. Google Maps shapes traffic patterns (by routing millions of drivers simultaneously), neighbourhood desirability (areas that "look" accessible and well-connected on the map attract more interest), tourism (landmarks that appear prominently get more visitors, which makes them more "important," which makes them appear more prominently), and even property values.

When two billion people use the same model of reality to make daily decisions, the model becomes reality. The map eats the territory. Korzybski is spinning in his grave.

All Models Are Wrong, Some Models Are Useful, One Model Is Google

The statistician George Box famously said: "All models are wrong, but some are useful." This is the scientist's version of Korzybski's insight. Every model — every map, every equation, every theory, every neural network — is a simplification. It throws away information to make the remaining information manageable.

A street map throws away building heights, weather, the mood of the people walking around, the smell of the chai stall on the corner, the fact that there's a really aggressive dog near the municipal office that you should probably cross the road to avoid. It keeps roads and locations. That's the deal. You accept the information loss in exchange for navigability.

"The moment a model becomes good enough that people stop questioning it, it becomes more dangerous than a bad model — because bad models get challenged."

The problem arises when the model is so good, so seamless, so integrated into daily life, that people forget it's a model at all. And that's exactly what's happened with Google Maps. Nobody opens Google Maps and thinks "I am about to consult a simplified, lossy, algorithmically curated representation of physical space that necessarily omits vast amounts of information and encodes specific biases in its presentation." They think "I need to get to DMart."

But the biases are there. Google Maps makes choices about what to show and what to hide. It decides which businesses appear prominently and which are buried. It chooses colour schemes that emphasise certain features over others. It routes traffic based on algorithms that optimise for certain metrics (usually speed) while ignoring others (quiet streets, scenic routes, avoiding the road with the aggressive dog).

Every one of these choices is a place where the map diverges from the territory. And because two billion people are making decisions based on these choices, the divergences matter enormously.

Borges and the Perfect Map

In 1946, Jorge Luis Borges — the Argentine writer who basically invented literary philosophy as a genre — wrote a one-paragraph story called "On Exactitude in Science." It describes a fictional empire where the cartographers became so obsessed with accuracy that they eventually created a map the exact size of the territory it represented. A 1:1 scale map. Every hill, every river, every blade of grass, faithfully reproduced.

The map was, of course, completely useless. You can't navigate with a map that's the same size as the thing you're navigating. The whole point of a map is that it's smaller than reality. It compresses information. A perfect map — a map that captures every detail of the territory — is just... the territory again. You've gained nothing.

Borges' story is a reductio ad absurdum of the quest for perfect models. And it applies directly to the current obsession with AI "world models."

The ideal map would contain every detail of reality. But a map containing every detail of reality would be as complex as reality itself, which means it would be exactly as hard to navigate as reality itself. The value of a map comes precisely from what it leaves out. Compression is not a bug — it's the entire point.

AI World Models: Borges' Nightmare Comes True

In the AI research world, there's a concept called a "world model" — an internal representation of how the world works that an AI system uses to make predictions and plan actions. The dream, for some researchers, is to build AI systems with comprehensive world models: systems that understand physics, social dynamics, cause and effect, spatial relationships, and all the other things humans intuit effortlessly.2

But Borges' paradox applies here too. How detailed should a world model be?

If it's too simple, it makes bad predictions. A world model that doesn't understand gravity will suggest you can walk off cliffs. A world model that doesn't understand social norms will suggest you can cut the token queue at the government office. (To be fair, a world model that does understand government office queues might conclude that life is not worth living.)

If it's too detailed, it becomes computationally intractable. You end up simulating every atom, which is just... running a universe. You haven't made a model. You've made a thing.

The sweet spot — and this is the deep insight — is somewhere in between. A good model is one that captures the right simplifications. One that knows what to leave out. One that compresses reality into something manageable without losing the bits that matter for the task at hand.

Your Mental Map Is Also Not the Territory

Here's where this gets personal. Because it's not just cartographic maps and AI models that are "maps" in Korzybski's sense. Your entire perception of reality is a map.

Your brain doesn't have direct access to the world. It sits in a dark, silent box (your skull) and receives electrical signals from your sensory organs. From those signals, it constructs a model — a map — of what's out there. You never experience reality directly. You experience your brain's best guess about reality, updated in real time based on incoming data.

This model is extraordinarily good. It's so good that you don't notice it's a model. You think you're seeing the world as it is, but you're actually seeing your brain's heavily edited, compressed, assumption-laden reconstruction of the world. You're living inside a map and you don't know it.

And just like Google Maps, your mental map has biases. It over-represents things you're paying attention to and under-represents everything else. It fills in gaps with assumptions (which is why you can "see" the back of someone's head even though you're only looking at the front). It's biased towards threats, towards faces, towards things that move. It's a survival-optimised map, not an accuracy-optimised map.

Optical illusions are the clearest proof that your mental map diverges from the territory. When you see two lines that are the same length but look different (the Muller-Lyer illusion), that's your brain's map getting the territory wrong. When you see a white-and-gold dress (or is it blue-and-black?), that's two people's maps disagreeing about the same territory. When you walk into a room and forget why you went in there — that's your map failing to preserve continuity during a location change, a phenomenon psychologists call the "doorway effect."

We are all, always, living in the map. The territory is out there somewhere, presumably, but we never actually visit it.

The Danger of the Good-Enough Map

So what's the takeaway from all this?

I think it's this: the most dangerous map is not the inaccurate one. It's the one that's accurate enough to be trusted completely.

An obviously bad map gets questioned. If your sat-nav routes you into a lake, you override it (most of the time — though there are genuinely horrifying news stories about people who didn't). The badness of the model is visible, so you maintain critical distance.

But a map that's right 98% of the time? That's the dangerous one. Because you stop checking. You stop looking out the window. You stop comparing the model to reality. You follow it blindly, and the 2% of the time it's wrong, you're not prepared.

This applies to Google Maps. It applies to AI models, which are becoming so fluid and confident that people are starting to trust their outputs without verification. It applies to financial models, which were accurate enough to be trusted right up until 2008, when the territory diverged from the map so violently that the global economy nearly collapsed. And it applies to our own mental models — our assumptions about how the world works, what people are like, what we're capable of.

The practice of periodically asking "is my map still accurate?" is one of the most valuable cognitive habits a person can develop. Scientists call it "updating your priors." Philosophers call it "epistemic humility." Everyone else calls it "being open-minded," which sounds nicer but means the same thing: holding your model of reality loosely enough that you can change it when reality sends you a correction.

Korzybski's warning — the map is not the territory — is ultimately a call for intellectual humility. It's a reminder that every model, no matter how sophisticated, is a lossy compression of something richer and more complex. The street map loses the smell of the bakery. The financial model loses the panic of real humans. The AI world model loses... well, we're still figuring out what it loses, but it definitely loses something.

And Google Maps? Google Maps is the map that got so good it started editing the territory. It's the model that became the thing it was modelling. It's Borges' nightmare and Korzybski's warning, rolled into a free app on your phone that's also, somehow, better at navigating Mumbai than any auto-rickshaw driver who ever lived.

The map is not the territory. But it's getting closer every day. And we should probably keep an eye on that.

Especially near the municipal office with the dog.
