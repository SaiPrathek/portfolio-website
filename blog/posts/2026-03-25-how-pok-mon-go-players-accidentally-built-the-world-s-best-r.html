The Accidental Army of Cartographers

Here's what happened: for ten years, approximately 500 million people1 downloaded PokÃ©mon Go and proceeded to walk around their neighbourhoods, parks, cities, and shopping centres while their phones quietly recorded everything. The paths they took. The obstacles they avoided. The shortcuts they discovered. The places where footpaths actually exist versus where Google Maps thinks they exist (spoiler: these are often hilariously different).

Every time someone walked around a bollard, their phone noted: "humans don't walk through bollards." Every time someone took a diagonal across a park instead of following the path, their phone noted: "humans are efficient and will absolutely ignore that 'keep off the grass' sign." Every time someone paused at a kerb, waited for traffic, then crossed at a spot with no zebra crossing, their phone noted: "this is where humans actually cross, not where the infrastructure suggests they should."

This data is, quite literally, priceless for anyone trying to build robots that navigate human spaces. Because here's the thing about robot navigation: it's not actually that hard to get a robot to move from point A to point B. The hard part is getting it to do so without terrifying everyone around it.

The Uncanny Valley of Movement

Humans are extraordinarily good at detecting when something is moving "wrong." We evolved this skill because things that move wrong are often things that are injured (potential food) or things that are pretending to be normal while actually being dangerous (potential predator). Our brains have a finely-tuned wrongness detector, and it fires constantly at early-generation robots.

Watch an early Boston Dynamics robot walk sometime. Technically, it's walking. Mechanically, it's perfect. But something in your lizard brain screams: that thing is not okay and I should leave.

The difference between a robot that can navigate and a robot that can navigate like a human is the difference between a cover letter written by ChatGPT and one written by an actual person. Both technically accomplish the task. Only one doesn't make you vaguely uncomfortable.

To move naturally through human spaces, robots need to learn the unwritten rules. The social physics. The fact that you don't walk directly at someone and expect them to move. That you slow down near children. That you give dogs a wider berth than adults. That you don't stop in doorways (a rule many humans have also failed to learn, but I digress).

This kind of knowledge cannot be programmed. It can only be learned. And the best way to learn it is to watch millions of humans doing it, over and over, in every possible context.

Enter: everyone who ever caught a Charizard.

The Beautiful Scam (That Wasn't Really a Scam)

They're a mapping company â€” they spun out of Google, for goodness' sake. The spatial data collection was always part of the deal.

But there's something philosophically interesting about the exchange that happened here.

"People gave away the most detailed pedestrian movement data ever collected, and in exchange, they got... happier?"

Because here's the thing: PokÃ©mon Go genuinely improved a lot of people's lives. Studies showed it increased physical activity. It got people out of the house. It created unexpected social connections. My uncle Abhishek lost 12 kilograms and made friends with a group of teenagers who taught him about "IV breeding" (I still don't know what this means, and I've chosen not to ask).

The Deeper Pattern

This isn't actually a story about PokÃ©mon Go. It's a story about a pattern that's becoming increasingly common: the gamification of data collection.

CAPTCHA made us label images to train AI while proving we weren't robots (irony noted). Duolingo uses our translations to build language models. Waze turns our commutes into traffic data. Amazon's recommendation engine learns from every click, every hover, every abandoned cart.

We are all, constantly, training machines. The question is whether we're doing it consciously.

I'm not trying to be alarmist. This isn't a "delete your apps" rant. I think most of these exchanges are genuinely fine, even beneficial. But I do think we should be aware of them.

Because the PokÃ©mon Go revelation isn't that Niantic did something nefarious. It's that they did something clever â€” so clever that most people didn't even realise they were part of it until a decade later.

The Desire Path Problem

There's a concept in urban planning called "desire paths" â€” those worn muddy trails across grass where people actually walk, as opposed to the paved paths where planners wanted them to walk. Every university campus has them. Every park. Every bit of green space between a parking area and a building entrance. They're the physical manifestation of the gap between how architects think humans should behave and how humans actually behave.

PokÃ©mon Go data revealed the digital equivalent of desire paths, but at a scale that urban planners could only dream about.

Think about it. Traditional methods for understanding pedestrian flow involve hiring people to stand at intersections with clipboards, or installing expensive sensors, or analysing grainy CCTV footage. You might get data on a few hundred people over a few days at a single location. PokÃ©mon Go gave researchers data on millions of people over years across entire cities. Every shortcut through an alley. Every jaywalking hotspot. Every place where the pavement is so bad that people walk in the road instead. Every desire path, mapped in exquisite detail, without anyone having to commission a single study.3

And this data isn't just useful for robotics â€” it's gold for city planning itself. Where should you put zebra crossings? Where people actually cross, not where the Highway Code says they should. Where should you widen pavements? Wherever the data shows pedestrians constantly stepping into the road because the current pavement is too narrow. Where should you put benches? Wherever people cluster and linger, which is often nowhere near where current benches are.

Urban planners have spent decades trying to answer the question: "How do people actually use this space?" PokÃ©mon Go players answered it by accident, in higher resolution than any planned study could achieve, while trying to hatch a virtual egg by walking 10 kilometres.

There's a wonderful irony here. Cities spend millions on "smart city" sensor networks to understand how people move through urban spaces. Meanwhile, Niantic collected better data for free because they understood something fundamental: you don't need sensors if you can convince people to be the sensors. You just need to give them a reason to turn their phones on and start walking.

The desire path data also reveals something deeper about the relationship between infrastructure and behaviour. We tend to assume that if you build infrastructure, people will use it as intended. Build a path, people walk on it. Build a crossing, people cross there. But the PokÃ©mon Go data shows just how wrong this assumption is. Humans are relentless optimisers. They will always find the most efficient route, the most convenient crossing point, the shortest line between where they are and where they want to be â€” and they will cheerfully ignore any infrastructure that doesn't align with those preferences.

This has profound implications for how we design cities, buildings, and public spaces. But it also has implications for how we design digital spaces, software interfaces, and yes, robot behaviour. Because if a delivery robot follows the "correct" path while every human around it is taking the shortcut, that robot isn't going to blend in. It's going to look like the one person at a party who actually uses the front door instead of cutting through the garden like everyone else.

The Mechanical Turk Problem

Let's talk about something uncomfortable.

In the late 18th century, a Hungarian inventor named Wolfgang von Kempelen built a chess-playing machine called "The Turk." It was a mechanical automaton that could beat nearly any human opponent. It toured Europe, defeating Napoleon and Benjamin Franklin. It was, by every appearance, artificial intelligence two centuries before the term existed.

It was also, of course, a fraud. Inside the cabinet, hidden by an ingenious arrangement of gears and mirrors, sat a human chess master who operated the machine.

We have not moved on from this as much as we'd like to believe.

Amazon literally named its crowdsourcing platform "Mechanical Turk" â€” a service where thousands of humans perform tiny tasks that computers can't do well: labelling images, transcribing audio, moderating content, categorising products. The entire pitch is right there in the name: artificial artificial intelligence. Human labour, dressed up to look like machine intelligence.

Content moderation at social media companies? Largely humans, sitting in offices in the Philippines and Kenya, watching the worst things the internet has to offer for wages that would make you wince. Self-driving car training data? Annotated by armies of gig workers drawing bounding boxes around pedestrians, cyclists, and traffic lights, frame by frame, for hours on end. That voice assistant that seems so clever? Its responses were refined by thousands of human raters who evaluated outputs and corrected errors.

So much of what we call "AI" is really just human labour with extra steps.

And PokÃ©mon Go might be the most elegant example of this phenomenon in history. Millions of humans, performing the labour of pedestrian navigation data collection â€” work that would normally require trained surveyors, expensive equipment, and months of fieldwork â€” and doing it for free. Not even for free, actually. They paid for the privilege. In-app purchases for the game totalled billions of dollars. People paid money to do work they didn't know they were doing.

Now, is this exploitation? I keep turning this question over in my head, and I keep landing on: it's complicated.

On one hand, Niantic created genuine value for players. The game was fun. It improved health outcomes. It built communities. Nobody was coerced. The terms of service were available to read (though let's be honest, nobody reads terms of service â€” another form of the Mechanical Turk problem, now that I think about it).

On the other hand, there's something uncomfortable about a company generating billions of dollars' worth of spatial data from labour that people didn't know they were performing. The informed consent question here is genuinely tricky. Can you consent to something you don't know is happening? Legally, yes â€” you agreed to the terms. Ethically? That's a much harder question.

I think the honest answer is that PokÃ©mon Go sits in a grey zone that we don't have good language for yet. It's not exploitation in the traditional sense â€” nobody was harmed, and most people got something valuable out of it. But it's also not a fully transparent exchange. It's something new. A kind of ambient labour where the work happens as a side effect of play, and the worker never knows they're working.

And here's the really unsettling bit: this model is spreading. Every app that collects behavioural data is, in some sense, a Mechanical Turk. Every time you scroll, tap, pause, or swipe, you're performing micro-labour that trains a model somewhere. The question isn't whether this is happening â€” it is. The question is whether we're going to develop norms and frameworks for thinking about it, or whether we're just going to keep catching Pikachu and hoping for the best.

What This Means for You (Yes, You)

Three takeaways, because I'm contractually obligated to make this practical:

1. Your behaviour has value beyond the obvious. When you use any app, you're not just consuming a service. You're generating data about how humans behave. This data has downstream applications you cannot predict. This isn't necessarily bad â€” it's just true.

2. Fun is a powerful motivator for collective action. Niantic couldn't have paid people to walk around collecting this data. It would have been prohibitively expensive and the data would have been worse (paid workers take shortcuts; players trying to catch rare PokÃ©mon are thorough). The lesson: if you want humans to do something at scale, make it genuinely enjoyable. This applies to everything from fitness apps to civic engagement.

3. The future is being built in weird ways. Ten years ago, if you'd told robotics researchers that their biggest breakthrough in pedestrian navigation would come from a game about catching pocket monsters, they would have laughed. But here we are. The future rarely arrives through the front door. It sneaks in through the games we play, the shortcuts we take, the silly apps we download because we're bored on the train.2

The Punchline

I called my uncle Abhishek while writing this piece. Told him about the robot navigation thing. There was a long pause.

"So you're telling me," he said slowly, "that all those hours I spent walking around in the heat, trying to catch a bloody Snorlax outside DMart... I was teaching robots to walk?"

"Basically, yes."

Another pause.

"Well," he said finally, "at least the robots will know not to stop in the middle of the pavement. I taught them that. I walked around those people very aggressively."

And honestly? That might be Abhishek's greatest contribution to the future of artificial intelligence. Somewhere, in a training dataset, there's a data point that says: "When humans encounter other humans who have stopped inexplicably in a walkway, they navigate around them with what can only be described as passive-aggressive velocity."

The robots will learn. The robots will understand.

The robots will be just as annoyed as the rest of us.
