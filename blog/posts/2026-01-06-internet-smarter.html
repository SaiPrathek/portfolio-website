The Internet Was Supposed to Make Us Smarter and I Have the Graph to Prove It Didn't

All of human knowledge, in your pocket. Used primarily to settle chai tapri arguments and watch cats fall off tables.

Internet AI Culture

In 1995, Nicholas Negroponte — co-founder of the MIT Media Lab, professional futurist, owner of an extremely confident jawline — published Being Digital. In it, he predicted that the internet would democratise knowledge, eliminate ignorance, and usher in a golden age of informed citizenry.

It is now 2026. The most-searched question on Google last year was "what time is the IPL match today." The third was "is a hot dog a sandwich."1

Something went wrong. Let's talk about what.

The Promise vs. The Reality

The optimistic vision of the internet went something like this: give everyone access to the sum total of human knowledge, and they'll use it to educate themselves, engage with complex ideas, and become better-informed participants in democracy and public discourse.

The reality went like this: give everyone access to the sum total of human knowledge, and they'll use it to watch a man get hit in the groin with a football, argue about vaccines in a comments section, and develop a parasocial relationship with a stranger who opens toys on camera.

This isn't a failure of the internet. It's a failure of the assumption that access to information equals consumption of information. Which is like assuming that owning a gym membership equals being fit. Technically, the gym is right there. Practically, you haven't been in seven months.

Sturgeon's Law and the Content Tsunami

Theodore Sturgeon was a science fiction author who, in 1957, responded to criticism that "90% of science fiction is crud" by pointing out that 90% of everything is crud. This became known as Sturgeon's Law, and it is the single most useful heuristic for understanding the internet.

The internet didn't change the ratio. It changed the volume. If 90% of everything is garbage and you go from 1,000 things to 10,000,000,000 things, you now have 9,000,000,000 pieces of garbage to wade through. The 10% that's brilliant is still out there, but it's buried under a landfill of content that ranges from "mildly misleading" to "actively harmful" to "a 45-minute video essay about the lore of a children's restaurant chain."

"The internet didn't make information scarce or abundant. It made attention scarce. And whoever controls what you pay attention to controls what you know, what you believe, and what you buy."

This is the core shift. Before the internet, the bottleneck was information supply. After the internet, the bottleneck is attention. And the entire economy of the modern web is structured around competing for that attention, which means the content that wins isn't the most true, the most useful, or the most educational. It's the most engaging. And "engaging" usually means "emotionally activating," which usually means "outrage, fear, novelty, or cats."

The Dunning-Kruger Internet

Here's something that Negroponte didn't anticipate: the internet doesn't just give everyone access to information. It gives everyone a platform. A megaphone. A stage. And crucially, it gives that stage regardless of whether you have anything sensible to say.

Before the internet, if you wanted to broadcast your opinion to thousands of people, you had to clear some hurdles. You needed a publisher, or a newspaper column, or at minimum a very large sign and a willingness to stand on a street corner. These hurdles weren't perfect filters for quality, but they were something. They created friction. And friction, it turns out, was doing a lot of the heavy lifting.

The internet removed the friction. All of it. Now a Nobel laureate in epidemiology and a guy called Raju who "did his own research" on YouTube for forty-five minutes have the same publishing tools, the same reach, and — this is the important bit — often the same apparent authority. In fact, Raju might have more reach, because Raju is confident, speaks simply, and posts twelve times a day. The Nobel laureate hedges, uses caveats, and publishes once a quarter. The algorithm does not reward caveats.

This is the Dunning-Kruger effect scaled to civilisational proportions. The people who know the least about a topic are the most confident in their opinions about it, and the internet amplifies confidence. It's a megaphone that doesn't check credentials. The result is an information environment where the loudest voices are, systematically, the least informed ones.

"It takes one sentence to make a wrong claim. It takes a paragraph to debunk it. It takes a thread to debunk it convincingly. And by then, nobody's still reading."

This asymmetry has a name: Brandolini's Law, also known as the bullshit asymmetry principle. It states that the amount of energy required to refute nonsense is an order of magnitude larger than the amount required to produce it. Alberto Brandolini came up with this in 2013, presumably after spending an afternoon on Twitter, and it remains the most precise description of online discourse ever written.

Think about how this plays out in practice. Someone posts "vaccines cause autism" — six words, takes two seconds to type. Debunking that claim properly requires explaining the original Wakefield study, its retraction, the subsequent two decades of research involving millions of participants, the difference between correlation and causation, and the base rates of autism diagnosis. That's a 2,000-word essay. And the person who posted the six words will simply reply "lol do your own research" and move on to their next claim.

The confidently wrong are playing a different game to the cautiously right. They're playing speed chess. The experts are playing correspondence chess. And the audience — scrolling at pace, attention fractured, looking for simple stories — overwhelmingly watches the speed chess.

The internet was supposed to surface the best ideas through the marketplace of ideas. What it actually created was a marketplace where the cheapest products are displayed most prominently and the high-quality goods are in a locked cabinet at the back that requires a library card to open.

The Paradox of Choice, Knowledge Edition

There's a parallel here to the Netflix problem. When you have infinite entertainment options, you end up watching nothing. When you have infinite information, you end up learning nothing. The mechanism is the same: the sheer volume overwhelms your decision-making capacity, so you default to whatever requires the least cognitive effort.

This is why the most-consumed content on the internet is short-form video. Not because people prefer it intrinsically, but because it has the lowest decision cost. You don't choose a TikTok. TikTok chooses you. It removes the burden of selection entirely, replacing it with an algorithm that serves you a continuous stream of content calibrated to your dopamine response.

"We didn't get the Library of Alexandria in everyone's pocket. We got the Library of Alexandria in everyone's pocket, on fire, surrounded by people shouting their opinions about the fire."

The Google Effect and the Death of Memory

In 2011, a psychologist named Betsy Sparrow published a paper in Science that should have alarmed everyone and instead barely made a ripple. She demonstrated, through a series of clever experiments, that when people know they can look something up later, they don't bother remembering it. They remember where to find the information, but not the information itself. She called this the "Google effect," and if you're reading this on a device connected to the internet — which you are, obviously, because this is a webpage — it's happening to you right now.

Think about it. When was the last time you memorised a phone number? A postal address? The capital of a country you'd never been to? The year a historical event happened? You don't need to memorise any of that, because Google is always there. Your phone is always there. The answer is always ten seconds away. So why would you use precious mental real estate to store it?

Sparrow's framing was actually quite neutral. She compared the internet to a form of "transactive memory" — the same kind of cognitive outsourcing that happens when you rely on a spouse or colleague to remember certain things. ("I don't know what our insurance policy number is, but my wife does.") We've always distributed memory across people. Now we're distributing it across machines.

The optimistic reading is that this is fine. Good, even. If you don't have to waste cognitive resources remembering that the Battle of Hastings was in 1066, you can use those resources for higher-order thinking. Analysis. Synthesis. Creativity. The boring factual stuff gets handled by the machine, and you get to do the interesting work on top.

The pessimistic reading is that this is making us intellectually flabby. That memory isn't just storage — it's the raw material for thought. You can't have a flash of insight connecting two ideas if you've outsourced both ideas to a search engine. You can't think critically about a historical pattern if you can't remember any history. Knowledge isn't a cupboard you open when you need something. It's the furniture of your mind. It shapes what thoughts are even possible.

The honest answer is probably: both. We're simultaneously freeing up cognitive bandwidth and losing the substrate that makes deep thinking possible. We're becoming faster at finding information and slower at understanding it. We're brilliant at retrieving and terrible at retaining.

And there's a subtler problem. When you know you can look something up, you develop a kind of false confidence in your own knowledge. You feel like you know things, because the boundary between "I know this" and "I can Google this in three seconds" has become invisible. This is the illusion of knowledge, and the internet has made it universal. We all walk around feeling like experts because we have an expert in our pocket. But the expert is a phone, and we are just the person holding it.

The Filter Bubble Problem

In 2011 — a big year for people trying to warn us about the internet, apparently — Eli Pariser gave a TED talk and published a book called The Filter Bubble. His argument was simple and, in retrospect, painfully prescient: the algorithms that curate your online experience are designed to show you more of what you already like, click on, and agree with. This creates an invisible bubble around you, a personalised reality that reinforces your existing beliefs and shields you from anything that might challenge them.

The internet was supposed to be the great connector. The thing that exposed a farmer in Iowa to the perspectives of a student in Lagos, a pensioner in Kyoto, a programmer in Berlin. And in theory, that's all still possible. In practice, the farmer in Iowa sees content about farming in Iowa, and the programmer in Berlin sees content about programming in Berlin, and neither of them ever encounters the other's worldview because the algorithm has determined it wouldn't maximise engagement.

This is how you end up with people living in completely different informational realities. Not because the internet lacks diverse viewpoints — it has more diverse viewpoints than any medium in human history — but because the systems that mediate your access to the internet have sorted you into a tribe and are now feeding you a steady diet of tribal reinforcement.

The mechanism is insidious because it's invisible. You don't see the articles you're not being shown. You don't notice the perspectives that have been algorithmically pruned from your feed. You just see a version of reality that feels comprehensive — because it fills your entire screen — but is actually a carefully curated slice designed to keep you comfortable and scrolling.

"The internet didn't expose us to the full range of human thought. It identified which narrow band of human thought would keep us clicking and then cranked the volume on that band until it was all we could hear."

Pariser's concern was fundamentally about democracy. If citizens don't share a common set of facts, how do they debate policy? If everyone's information environment is personalised to confirm their priors, how does anyone change their mind? The answer, increasingly, is that they don't. People don't argue about the interpretation of facts anymore. They argue about which facts are real. That's a much harder problem, and it's one the internet didn't create so much as industrialise.

The cruel irony is that the more information the internet contains, the narrower your slice of it becomes. The algorithm scales with the data. More content means more precise filtering, which means tighter bubbles. The Library of Alexandria, it turns out, has a personalised entrance for each visitor, and each entrance leads to a different library.

Can AI Fix This?

The optimistic take is that AI — specifically, AI-powered curation — might be the thing that finally delivers on the original promise. Instead of wading through 9 billion pieces of garbage to find the good stuff, you let an AI do the filtering. It knows what you've read, what you've learned, what gaps exist in your knowledge, and what would be most valuable for you to read next.

In theory, this is the "personalised tutor for everyone" vision that people like Sal Khan have been promoting. An AI that doesn't just serve you what's engaging, but what's educational. What fills gaps. What challenges you appropriately. What makes you smarter.

The pessimistic take is that AI will make this worse, not better. Because the same companies building AI are the ones whose business model depends on attention capture. An AI that makes you smarter might also make you spend less time on the platform. That's bad for revenue. An AI that keeps you scrolling forever is much more profitable, even if it leaves you dumber and more anxious.

What Actually Works

The honest answer is boring. The internet is a tool, and like any tool, it does what you use it for. If you use a hammer to hit yourself in the face, that's not the hammer's fault, but also maybe someone should have mentioned that's not what it's for.

The people who actually get smarter from the internet do a few things differently. They curate aggressively — following specific people, subscribing to specific newsletters, reading specific publications. They time-box their consumption — thirty minutes of reading, then they stop. They write about what they read, because writing forces comprehension in a way that scrolling doesn't.2

They treat the internet as a library, not a casino. Both are technically open to everyone. One makes you smarter. The other is designed to keep you in your seat.

The internet was supposed to make us smarter. It gave us the tools. Whether we use them is — infuriatingly, as always — up to us.
